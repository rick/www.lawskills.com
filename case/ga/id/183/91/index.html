Lawskills.com Georgia Caselaw
COLVIN v. COLVIN.
18892.
(211 Ga. 592)
(87 SE2d 390)
(1955)
Divorce. Before Judge Vaughn. DeKalb Superior Court. February 4, 1955.
MOBLEY, Justice.
1. The charge of the trial court defining the word cohabitation was error, in that it limited cohabitation to living in the same or common place of abode of the husband and wife.
2. Since the ruling above requires a judgment of reversal, and since the alleged error in recharging the jury will not likely occur on another trial, no ruling will here be made on the question of whether the instruction given in recharging the jury amounted to the direction of a verdict.
3. The evidence, though conflicting on the question of whether there had been cohabitation as husband and wife, was sufficient to support the verdict in favor of the husband.
Samuel E. Colvin filed in DeKalb Superior Court, against Mrs. Margaret Bruns Colvin, a petition for divorce, in which he alleged substantially the following: He separated from his wife on December 25, 1951, because of her cruel treatment towards him over a period of years. Subsequently, the parties on two occasions sought to reconcile their differences, but in each instance the result was a complete failure. Under a decree of Fulton Superior Court, dated January 29, 1953, custody of their minor daughter was awarded to the wife, custody of their two minor sons was awarded to the husband, and he was required to pay $60 per month alimony for the support of the wife and minor daughter.
Upon the question of whether there had been a reconciliation and recohabitation, the wife testified in part: She lived on Glendale Avenue in Decatur from January until June, 1953, during which period she and her husband tried to effect a reconciliation, and they cohabited as man and wife in her apartment. For three months he was at her apartment practically every night. Usually he would stay until the last bus left, and he spent the night a good many times. She and her husband had sexual intercourse as man and wife on the average of three or four times a week at least. It was either after their daughter had gone to sleep or gone out. She believed at that time that her husband was coming back and was going to make a home for her. She came to Atlanta hoping she could get him to make a home for all of them. The last reconciliation attempt ended about June, 1953, when he told her he had decided there was no use, that he was not going to live with her any more. He did not come to her apartment after June 30, 1953. After January 1, 1953, he was there nearly every day for three months; either that or he had her over to his apartment. He came alone most of the time, but sometimes he brought little George. He was visiting her every day during February, March, May, and part of June, 1953. He was sending her some support then around the 1st and 15th of each month.
The husband testified: From January 30, 1953, up to the present time, there had been no cohabitation between him and his wife, and they had not lived together. The reason he stayed as late as he did, his wife had a television set. Both they and the children became interested in watching wrestling matches and movies, which ran until around 12:30 a.m. Since January 30, 1953, he had not spent the night in his wife's apartment.
The jury returned a verdict granting a total divorce between the parties, and finding that the husband be allowed to remarry, and that the wife also be allowed to remarry.
Within thirty days of the divorce decree, the wife made a motion to modify or set aside the verdict and decree, assigning error on two excerpts from the charge, and upon the usual general grounds. To an order denying this motion the wife excepted.
1. The wife complains in her motion to modify or set aside the verdict and decree, that the trial court erred in instructing the jury. "Now the court charges you with respect to recohabitation, that if the parties in good faith effected a reconciliation and intended to live together as husband and wife, there is a recohabitation. Cohabitation by a man and woman as husband and wife is living in the same or common place of abode. The assumption of privileges and benefits, and this may include participation in sexual relationships although the mere casual acts of sexual intercourse are not conclusive evidence of a reconciliation, but such acts if there be such, may be taken in consideration by the jury along with the other testimony, circumstances and facts in the case in arriving at their findings as to whether the parties in this case have recohabited since January 30, 1953. There must be an intention between the parties to recohabitate."
The criticism is: (1) The charge was erroneous, misleading, and confusing to the jury and an incorrect statement of the law with respect to recohabitation, in that the court defined it as the husband and wife living in the same or common place of abode. (2) The wife contends that the parties had recohabited, in that the husband had spent the night in her apartment and had spent several hours therein, and that they had relations as husband and wife, but that, due to limited facilities, it was not possible to dwell together in said apartment, and that the charge took from the consideration of the jury the question of cohabitation under the circumstances quoted and as contended by the wife.
The Code, 30-109, declares: "If there has been a voluntary condonation and cohabitation subsequently to the acts complained of, and with notice thereof, then no divorce shall be granted." And Code 30-217 declares: "The subsequent voluntary cohabitation of the husband and wife shall annul and set aside all provision made, either by deed or decree, for permanent alimony."
Cohabitation has been defined by the courts to mean "to dwell together" or "a dwelling together." Phinizy v. Phinizy, 154 Ga. 199, 215 (114 S. E. 185); Wilson v. Wilson, 174 Ga. 238 (2) (162 S. E. 540). In 26 Am. Jur. 635, 7, the author said: ". . . By the matrimonial contract husband and wife are to live together. The term 'living together,' as applied to husband and wife, does not always mean a common place of living, but means living as husband and wife with voluntary recognition of the relationship, and no design or agreement to live apart with the chief end in view that of living apart free from reciprocal marital rights and duties; and on the other hand, the term 'living apart,' has applied to the spouses, does not always mean that they are not living in the same place, for even though they reside at the same place; they may not be living there as husband and wife, and hence, in such sense they are 'living apart.' " See also "cohabit" as defined in 14 C. J. S. 1311.
In Westberry v. Westberry, 173 Ga. 42 (1) (159 S. E. 671), it was held: "The allegation that the parties live in the same family dwelling, when taken in connection with the further allegation that they 'are living separate and apart,' is a sufficient allegation of living in a state of separation."
The definition given by the court, to wit, "Cohabitation by a man and woman as husband and wife is living in the same or common place of abode," is a requirement not necessarily contemplated in Code 30-217. There may be a voluntary cohabitation of a husband and wife without actually living in the same or common place of abode. If there is reconciliation in good faith upon the part of the husband and wife, and it is their intention to cohabit as husband and wife, and acting upon said reconciliation and intention, they do resume their marital status and live together as husband and wife, there is cohabitation within the meaning of the Code section above cited. The word "cohabitation" as used in the statute means the dwelling or being together as a man and wife. It does not, as above indicated, necessarily mean to live in the same or common place of abode, for these words are not an essential part of the definition.
Under the court's definition of cohabitation, the jury under the evidence in this case were not authorized to find that there had been cohabitation between the parties, although they might have believed the wife's testimony to the effect that there had been a dwelling together as husband and wife in a place other than "the same or common place of abode" of the husband and wife.
The error in defining the word "cohabitation" was therefore harmful to the wife, and requires the grant of a new trial. Wilson v. Wilson, 174 Ga. 238, 241 (162 S. E. 540); Powell v. Powell, 196 Ga. 694, 700 (27 S. E. 2d 393); Paris v. Paris, 197 Ga. 162, 164 (28 S. E. 2d 452); Powell v. Powell, 199 Ga. 723, 730 (35 S. E. 2d 298). 2, 3. The rulings announced in the second and third headnotes do not require elaboration.
DUCKWORTH, Chief Justice, dissenting. I dissent from the ruling in division 1 of the opinion and from the judgment of reversal.
Thomas T. Purdom, Grant, Wiggins, Grizzard & Smith, contra.
Walter McCurdy, Hubert C. Morgan, for plaintiff in error.
