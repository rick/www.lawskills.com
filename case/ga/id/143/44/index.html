JACKSON, Superintendent of Banks v. LONG.
25115.
(225 Ga. 227)
(167 SE2d 583)
(1969)
Injunction. Fulton Superior Court. Before Judge Pye.
DUCKWORTH, Chief Justice.
1. The provisions in Ga. L. 1966, pp. 692, 716 (Code Ann. Ch. 13-23), providing for the incorporation of regulated certificated banks, and Ga. L. 1966, p. 691 (Code Ann. 13-204) requiring all private banks to become certificated and incorporated, and forbidding any uncertificated or unincorporated banks after January 1, 1968, are valid as against constitutional attacks of denying due process and equal protection, or taking private property for public purposes without first paying therefor.
2. Where the stipulations show that the defendant private hank has not been certificated or incorporated, and was operating as a private bank after January 1, 1968, a plain violation of the law is shown and it was error to refuse to enjoin such operations.
3. The Superintendent of Banks is charged by law with regulating banks and seeing that they comply with the law. He was therefore empowered to bring this suit to enjoin an operation of a private bank that had not been certificated and incorporated as the law requires.
692, 716; Code Ann. Ch. 13-23). The defendant in his answer, as amended, (1) denied the authority of the Superintendent of Banks to bring this action; (2) alleged he had made application as a private banker to become a State Certificated Bank under the above law but the Superintendent of Banks refused to grant a certificated bank charter or certificate as required by the law; and (3) attacked the legislation requiring him to cease doing business as a sole proprietor and become incorporated and meet the various requirements of law as being unconstitutional, null and void.
The case proceeded to an interlocutory hearing of the request for a temporary injunction based upon an affidavit and a stipulation of certain facts as follows: (1) the defendant is a private banker who commenced operations during December 1959, under the ownership of a predecessor; (2) registered with the Superintendent of Banks as a private banker in 1966; (3) filed an application for incorporation as a regulated certificated bank pursuant to said legislation approved in 1966, which was disapproved by the Superintendent under 11 (b) of the Act, all of which application and disapproval was attached to the stipulation as exhibits, showing a determination that the applicants were not actively engaged in the banking business. The constitutional attacks upon the legislation are based upon (1) denial of equal protection, (2) denial of due process, and (3) destruction of defendant's business without due compensation therefor. The court denied the request for injunction and held: (1) the parties agree that defendant is engaged in the private banking business; (2) by legislation the General Assembly has undertaken to prohibit all private banking and restrict the right to conduct such banking business to corporations; and (3) the legislation is therefore unconstitutional, null and void in denying the right to individuals to carry on a lawful business, thereby destroying the defendant's lawfully existing business without compensation. The appeal is from this judgment.
1. Anyone who has witnessed the agonies of a community caused by an insolvent bank's closing and liquidation will readily agree that banking affects the public interest and welfare, and hence can be regulated by law. The need for a bank, its chances of success, the character of its directors, and a means of keeping a constant public watch over it, at once justify legislation reasonably designed to meet these needs. Incorporation, where the capital is known, where more than one has authority in its operation to protect innocent depositors, where minutes are kept and an examination is easier, all tend to protect the public interest. Indeed this public interest is sufficiently important to justify the State in prohibiting banking in any other manner. Therefore, Ga. L. 1966, pp. 691, 692, is constitutional and valid; likewise, Ga. L. 1966, pp. 692, 716, prescribing terms and conditions for private banks, and prohibiting their operations after a certain date, is for the same reason valid and does not offend "due process," "equal protection" and is not the "taking private property for public purposes without first paying therefor," under either the State or the Federal Constitution. It is noted that the statute, whether or not it could constitutionally do so, does not put a private bank out of business. It merely subjects it to conditions that enable the State to examine and control it to the extent necessary to safeguard the public. All this is supported by Dillingham v. McLaughlin, 264 U. S. 370 (44 SC 362, 68 LE 742); Noble State Bank v. Haskell, 219 U. S. 104 (31 SC 186, 55 LE 112); Harrison v. Hartford Steam Boiler Inspection &c. Co., 183 Ga. 1 (187 SE 648); Austin v. Augusta Terminal R., 108 Ga. 671 (34 SE 852, 47 LRA 755); Cooper v. Rollins, 152 Ga. 588 (110 SE 726, 20 ALR 1105); 16 AmJur2d 626, Constitutional Law, 322; 16 CJS 925, 935, Constitutional Law, 188, 189. We therefore reverse the ruling that either of the 1966 Acts, supra, is unconstitutional.
2. Now we have left Section 48 of Ga. L. 1966, pp. 692, 715 (Code Ann. 13-2348) and the stipulated admission that the defendant was operating a private bank after January 1, 1968, without having a charter and certification to conduct a banking business, in direct conflict with that provision of the law. It is contended that since that section begins with requiring an application within six months of the effective date of the Act, July 1, 1966, and the defendant is conceded to have made application, he is not violating the law even though his application was disapproved and he was not certificated or incorporated under the law. We concede that if sound construction would allow us to isolate this sentence from the entire Act and construe it alone, it would mean "application" alone as a requirement, but this cannot be done. It must be construed along with the entire Act, and the legislative intent as disclosed by the entire Act must be given effect. Section II (b) (Code Ann. 13-2311; Ga. L. 1966, pp. 692, 699) allows a six-month period in which to comply with the requirements set forth by the superintendent, and all statutes with reference to private banks are specifically repealed eighteen months after the effective date of the Act on July 1, 1966, thereby making all private banks cease operating by January 1, 1968, unless they have obtained a charter or are certificated banks. It would be empty and futile to require an application, and, when it is justifiably denied, permit the private bank to continue operation, thus defeating the sole purpose of the law. We therefore hold that a private bank must get certificated as provided by the Act by January 1, 1968, or cease doing a banking business within six months after disapproval of its application.
3. But it is contended that the superintendent is not authorized to bring this action. The duties placed upon him to protect the public against unsafe banks, and the expressed authority to sue to enforce the banking law fully clothe him with power to bring this suit. Code Ann. 13-2306 (Ga. L. 1966, pp. 692, 696). We have not dealt with the superintendent's disapproval of the application and his reasons therefor, since the defendant is clearly operating contrary to law. Whether or not he could by mandamus compel the superintendent to perform his duties if he was dissatisfied with his refusal to approve the application is not here involved.
It follows that the trial court erred in rendering the judgment appealed from.
Long & Glean, Nick Long, Jr., for appellee.
Arthur K. Bolton, Attorney General, J. Robert Coleman, Robert J. Castellani, Assistant Attorneys General, for appellant.
