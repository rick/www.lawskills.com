ROWLAND v. THE STATE.
35416.
(91 Ga. App. 221)
(85 SE2d 522)
(1954)
Receiving stolen goods. Before Judge Stephens. Johnson Superior Court. August 14, 1954.
GARDNER, P. J.
The defendant was convicted by a jury of receiving stolen goods under an indictment which charged the offense of felony "For that the said Fred Rowland . . . did unlawfully buy and receive from some person to the grand jury unknown [said described property] of the value of $175 . . . the said Fred Rowland knowing said goods were stolen when he so bought and received them . . ." He filed his motion for a new trial on the general grounds only.
The evidence shows substantially the following: That Braswell's Grill was entered by forcing a padlock on the door, the exact time of the breaking being unknown; that the property stolen was one adding machine, cigarettes and salmon, and sausage; all of a value of $289; that the sheriff, on information received from G. B. I. men, found buried in a corn crib belonging to the defendant, and recovered from the possession of the defendant the adding machine, 28 cartons of cigarettes, 28 cans of salmon, one pair of scales and a radio; that the defendant stated he knew the adding machine was a stolen machine and said he hid this machine and other property in the corn when he heard that the sheriff was looking for it until he could contact the Negro he bought it from and demand the return of his money. The sheriff testified that one or more people were present when the defendant made this admission; that the defendant stated he bought the adding machine and scales from a Negro named Lemon, and bought the "other stuff" from another party; that the defendant made the admission without intimidations; that the sheriff investigated a Negro named Lemon, but found that the Negro knew nothing of the goods stolen; that Braswell could not identify the cigarettes or the salmon, but he did identify the adding machine; that at about the same time the store of Sarah Sumner was broken into, and goods stolen being of the value of $300 that were taken therefrom. Among other things was a radio which was recovered in the corn at the defendant's residence; that the officers recovered a pair of scales taken from another store; that Braswell's store was entered by breaking a window.
Sarah Sumner testified for the State that her grocery store was broken into; that the burglar or burglars pried open a shutter and broke a window to gain entrance, that she lost some dry goods, groceries and a radio; that the radio was recovered and properly identified by the witness. (It will be remembered that the sheriff testified that this radio was recovered from where it had been hidden under corn in the crib of the defendant.)
The defendant made a statement which included, among other things, the following: "I'm not claiming they didn't find this stuff in my crib, which I'm not doubting they did, but how it got there I don't know . . ." The defendant indicated that he was framed and was threatened.
While the bill of exceptions mentions one special ground, no special ground appears in the record. Also, both the defendant and the State make the following identical statements in their respective briefs: "The defendant filed a motion for a new trial on the general grounds, such motion was overruled and the defendant excepted." This court will therefore consider the special ground to which reference is made in the bill of exceptions as being abandoned and proceed to deal with this case on the general grounds only. We have heretofore set out all the pertinent part of the evidence.
The defendant cites several cases to the effect that they constitute the offense of receiving stolen goods, and that the person receiving the goods must have knowledge that the goods were stolen at the time of receiving the goods. Those cases are Von Sprecken v. State, 70 Ga. App. 222 (28 S. E. 2d 341) and Pat v. State, 116 Ga. 92 (42 S. E. 389). Counsel also cites Bird v. State, 72 Ga. App. 843 (5) (35 S. E. 2d 483), wherein the same principle was announced and wherein this court reversed the conviction, using the following language: "Where the testimony did not show any knowledge on the part of the accused that the shirt had been stolen at the time he received it, nor any circumstances from which the jury could do more than surmise the existence of such guilty knowledge on his part , the verdict was, without evidence to support it and contrary to law, and the court erred in overruling the motion for new trial." (Italics ours). In Von Sprecken v. State, supra, that case was reversed on a special ground and therefore the court did not pass on the general grounds. That case has no application to the instant case. In Pat v. State, supra, the court reversed the case upon a charge of the court regarding knowledge that goods were stolen at the time the goods were stolen. There is nothing in that case to indicate that the ruling there (the facts not being set forth in the opinion), should be controlling under the facts of the instant case. In Bird v. State, supra, apparently there were no circumstances from which the jury could do more than surmise the existence of guilty knowledge that the goods were stolen when received. In the instant case the circumstances were such that the jury were convinced that the defendant had knowledge that the goods were stolen when he received them. In Arkwright v. State, 57 Ga. App. 221 (194 S. E. 876), under the circumstances much weaker than in the instant case, this court affirmed the finding of the trial court, stating that the jury were authorized to deduce the knowledge on the part of the defendant at the time of receiving the stolen goods that the goods were stolen. The court in that case stated that guilt as to knowledge at the time of receiving stolen goods that the goods were stolen "may be inferred from circumstances," citing Birdsong v. State, 120 Ga. 850 (48 S. E. 329), and other cases. In the Birdsong case (quoting from Cobb v. State, 76 Ga. 666), the Supreme Court in affirming the conviction of the jury in the trial court stated: "The circumstances, the time, the secrecy, all the transactions before, at the time and afterwards, may be brought to bear upon what was the knowledge of the receiver; and if from all these the jury can conclude that the receiver did have good reason, as a reasonable person, to believe or suspect that the goods were stolen . . . knowledge may well be deduced from conduct and behavior . . ." This court is of the opinion that there is sufficient evidence that was given and circumstances to warrant the jury to find that the defendant had knowledge at the time the defendant received the stolen goods, that the goods were stolen.
The court did not err in denying the motion for a new trial for any of the reasons assigned.
W. W. Larsen, Solicitor-General, contra.
E. L. Rowland, Joe W. Rowland, J. Roy Rowland, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED NOVEMBER 23, 1954 -- REHEARING DENIED DECEMBER 3, 1954.
