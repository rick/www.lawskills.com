CARLYLE v. HUMBLE OIL & REFINING COMPANY
42038.
(114 Ga. App. 115)
(150 SE2d 299)
(1966)
Action on contract. Jefferson City Court. Before Judge Stark.
FELTON, Chief Judge.
1. Where a defendant, for value received, assigned to the plaintiff an account purportedly owed to the defendant on a series of charge tickets for merchandise purportedly delivered to the holder of a credit card, no recovery can be had by the plaintiff in a suit by it against the defendant upon said account upon proof that an unauthorized person or persons used said credit card to make said purchases and that the defendant failed to follow the instructions of the plaintiff issuer of the credit card as to indicating on the credit sales tickets the vehicle number, if any, the license tag number and the purchaser's signature.
Humble Oil & Refining Company brought an action on open account against L. E. Carlyle, the present appellant, who operated a filling station selling the products of the plaintiff-appellee. The following appears from the evidence: W. D. Bolton was the distributing agent for the plaintiff's products in the locality. Bolton (whether individually or as agent is not disclosed) sold products of the plaintiff to the defendant on account and received as payments on said account "in lieu of cash" credit card tickets for merchandise sold by the defendant to his customers on credit cards approved by the plaintiff. These were then sent to the plaintiff and the agent would receive a check from the plaintiff to cover, but in this particular instance the plaintiff did not send a check to cover because the credit card holder refused to pay the tickets. The account sued upon was composed of these tickets (attached as an exhibit to the petition) for merchandise purchased with an approved credit card issued to the Southeastern Transfer & Storage Company. There was evidence that the person, or persons, using the credit card were not authorized to do so, and that the products (gasoline mostly) were not placed in Southeastern's vehicles, this unauthorized use being the basis of Southeastern's refusal to pay.
The credit card issued to Southeastern was in the possession of one Fred Lollis, one of its employees authorized to sign for merchandise bought on the credit card, when he left work and was confined in a hospital during the time the transactions sued upon occurred. His purported signature, which was denied to be genuine by witnesses for Southeastern, appeared on most of the tickets. The evidence does not disclose whether or not the credit card was lost, remained in the possession of Lollis or was in someone else's possession. There is no dispute that the credit card was used to make the purchases in question before cancellation of the card or notice not to honor it.
Verdict and judgment were rendered in favor of the plaintiff and the defendant appealed from this judgment and the overruling of its motion for a new trial as amended.
The invoices which are the basis of the account sued upon are not against the defendant retailer, Carlyle, but against the holder of the credit card, Southeastern. There was no evidence of any kind to authorize a finding that the defendant was, by agreement or otherwise, legally bound to pay to the plaintiff the account sued upon, which was an account purportedly owed to the defendant by Southeastern and assigned by the defendant to the plaintiff or the plaintiff's agent for value received. Although the plaintiff introduced, without objection, evidence that the defendant had been orally instructed that the proper handling of sales to firms on credit cards was to indicate on the credit card tickets the vehicle number, if any, and the license tag number, and have the purchaser to sign, there was no evidence of any contract placing any liability on the defendant to the plaintiff for the payment of invoices on which said instructions were not followed. If, by reason of any agreement with the defendant, the plaintiff was authorized to charge the credit sales tickets, or invoices, back to the account of the defendant with the plaintiff (there was no evidence of any such agreement), this latter account should be the one sued upon and not the account evidenced by the tickets charged back. Although the plaintiff proved basically what it had alleged, i. e., that the account represented by the attached invoices was unpaid and apparently owing to it as assignee, in doing so it failed to introduce evidence which would make the defendant liable to the plaintiff on Southeastern's account.
Accordingly, the court erred in its judgment in favor of the plaintiff and in overruling the defendant's motion for a new trial on the general grounds. The special grounds are not ruled on as they are not likely to reoccur in another trial in view of the above opinion.
Judgment reversed. Frankum and Pannell, JJ., concur.
Davis & Davidson, Jack S. Davidson, for appellee.
Floyd G. Hoard, for appellant.
