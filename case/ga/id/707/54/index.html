FIDELITY & CASUALTY COMPANY et al. v. BROWN.
34731.
(89 Ga. App. 76)
(78 SE2d 618)
(1953)
Workmen's compensation. Before Judge Hendrix. Fulton Superior Court. May 8, 1953.
GARDNER, P. J.
This case originated as an appeal from an award by a hearing Director of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation, on March 5, 1953, granting compensation death benefits to Mrs. Lena Knight Brown (hereinafter called the claimant) because of the death of her husband, David Rosser Brown, on December 25, 1952, as an alleged result of a congestive heart failure which arose out of and in the course of his employment by Bradley's Body Works (hereinafter called the defendant) on December 12, 1952.
On March 10, 1953, the employer and insurance carrier filed their appeal to the full board on the sole ground "that the award of the single director is contrary to the law and evidence."
On March 25, 1953, the full board unanimously affirmed the award of the hearing director, and on March 31, 1953, the defendant appealed this award of the full board to the Superior Court of Fulton County, the appeal being based, in addition to the general grounds, upon three special grounds and containing a total of seven specific citations of alleged error.
On April 21, 1953, the case came on to be heard before a judge of Fulton Superior Court, on the appeal of the defendant. On May 8, 1953, the judge of Fulton Superior Court affirmed the ruling of the full board, thereby affirming the award of the hearing director. The defendant has now brought the bill of exceptions to this court, setting forth the same special grounds that were first raised in the Superior Court of Fulton County.
The testimony showed that the deceased was a painter for fifteen years with the defendant; that he was feeling very bad on the morning of December 12, 1952, the day he suffered his injury by accident, and further that he had not been feeling well for some time prior to this day; and that he died after a recurrent heart seizure on December 25, 1952.
The employer in this case testified that the deceased told him in the presence of a physician that he had been having trouble with shortness of breath and would have to get up in the middle of the night to see if he could get his breathing better from something like cramps. The testimony of Dr. David James, the attending physician of the deceased, was that he was an internist specializing in heart disease; that he first saw the deceased as a patient on December 12, 1952; and that his diagnosis at this time was "all these findings led to the conclusion that the patient was suffering from rheumatic heart disease with mitralstenosis, and was in a state of congestive heart failure."
Doctor James testified further that the death of the deceased on December 25th was from congestive heart failure due to mitralstenosis; and that, even if he had lived, he could not have returned to the type of work that he was doing without its being deleterious to his health. Dr. James testified further that the deceased had been suffering for the previous seven weeks from fatigue, shortness of breath, and a cough, and that the exertion of walking into the shop down the steep ramp and changing his clothes could have precipitated the heart attack. On cross-examination the doctor testified that the heart condition could have existed for a year, but to his knowledge extended over a period of six or seven weeks; and that in his opinion, it was not necessary to perform an autopsy because there was not sufficient reasonable doubt as to the valid connection between his recent and current illness and his death.
Dr. Maurice L. B. Clarke testified as an expert witness for the claimant. He testified that he treats heart disease; that, in his opinion, there would be a definite connection between the seizure and death in the case of the deceased; and that the exertion expended on the job on the morning of his first heart seizure would and did precipitate the heart attack.
Any doubt as to Dr. Clarke's expert opinion on this matter was cleared up on cross-examination, when he stated that any physical exertion in a man suffering mitralstenosis and rheumatic heart condition could bring on such a seizure, and the fact that it happened immediately after the exertion at work on the morning in question led him to believe that the exertion at the job precipitated the attack. Dr. Clarke testified in addition that the death several weeks after the initial seizure would be an expected result.
We have studied this evidence (hereinbefore set out somewhat in detail), in view of previous decisions of this court, and in view of the whole record. It is our opinion that the evidence of Dr. James alone, which is reiterated in part hereinbefore, is sufficient to sustain the finding of the single Director of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation, which finding was later affirmed by the full board, and subsequently affirmed by the Superior Court, of Fulton County. Certainly this evidence, coupled with other evidence from other competent sources, is ample to sustain such findings.
Moreover, there is nothing to show that the State Board of "Workmen's Compensation acted in excess of their powers in the award. While it is true that the evidence showed that the deceased suffered previously from a heart condition, it is also true that competent evidence showed that the condition was aggravated by exertion on the part of the employee while in the course of his employment.
The request that 10% damages be allowed is denied.
The judge of the superior court did not err in affirming the findings of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation that the deceased employee sustained an accidental injury which arose out of and in the course of his employment.
Judgment affirmed. Townsend and Carlisle, JJ., concur.
Currie & McGhee, contra.
John M. Slaton, J. Hugh Rogers, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED OCTOBER 14, 1953 -- REHEARING DENIED OCTOBER 29, 1953.
