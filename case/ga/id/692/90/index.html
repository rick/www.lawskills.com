Lawskills.com Georgia Caselaw
PITTS et al. v. BEGIN.
37026.
(96 Ga. App. 897)
(102 SE2d 50)
(1958)
Revocation of broker's license. Before Judge Alverson. Fulton Superior Court. October 29, 1957.
GARDNER, Presiding Judge.
Under the powers conferred by law upon the Georgia Real Estate Commission, when a broker sells property with the information and/or definite knowledge that another broker has an exclusive listing the Commission may revoke the license of the broker who disregarded the exclusive sales contract and proceeded to sell the property.
E. C. Eaton, a real-estate broker filed a complaint with the Georgia Real Estate Commission against Paul S. Begin, a real-estate broker. The Georgia Real Estate Commission at the time this complaint was brought was composed of Lum C. Pitts, chairman, and M. Duquince Davis and J. C. Bible, Jr., members. The complaint came on for hearing before the commission resulting in the following order: "The above matter coming on for a hearing September 12, 1956, Senate Chamber, State Capitol, Atlanta, Georgia. The complainant and defendant appearing with counsel and announcing ready. The defendant's attorney made a motion to dismiss the complaint on the grounds that the complaint did not legally set forth a charge upon which the defendant could be tried. The commission reserved its ruling upon said motion and proceeded to hear evidence both oral and documentary and heard argument of counsel and overruled motion of counsel and overruled motion to dismiss. The commission finds the defendant, Paul S. Begin, guilty of the charges as alleged, and hereby revokes his broker's license. This 12th day of September, 1956."
The defendant presented a petition for certiorari to a judge of the Superior Court of Fulton County. The petition was sanctioned, bond was posted, and a writ was issued against the commission to answer. The commission answered, the certiorari came on for hearing and, by agreement of counsel, was heard without evidence and submitted to the court on the pleadings and argument of counsel. At the hearing, counsel for the commission made an oral motion to dismiss the certiorari on the ground that the constitutional question raised by the certiorari was not raised at the hearing before the commission and further that the evidence sustained the finding of the commission to revoke the defendant's real-estate license. The judge of the superior court denied said motion and issued an order in which there was an admission that there was some evidence showing that the defendant might have known that the broker Eaton had an exclusive listing agreement signed by the seller of the house in question and that he might have been guilty of some of the violations with which he was charged, but the order set out that the court did not find the evidence sufficient to show him guilty of sufficient infractions of law to warrant the action of the commission in revoking his license. The judge ordered a new trial, and further ordered that the defendant's real-estate broker's license be reinstated pending the outcome of the said new trial. It is on this order that the case is here for review.
The evidence shows that the owners of the house in controversy testified that they told the defendant's agent that there was an exclusive sales contract good for ninety days made in favor of the complainant Eaton, and that the ninety days time limit had not elapsed; that the agent stated that the matter would be "made all right" with Eaton; that the owners told the defendant's agent that they did not want to get in any trouble, and the agent told them there was nothing to worry about. The defendant testified that this was not true; that he had no knowledge of an exclusive sales contract; that his agent told him that "when he [the agent] brought the listing from Mr. Maxwell, made some sort of statement to me that Mr. Maxwell had told him he had signed some sort of paper with Mr. Eaton. I didn't feel that it was my obligation to contact Mr. Eaton."
The evidence is somewhat conflicting. The commission resolved the evidence against the defendant. There was no jury trial. In addition to the evidence we have set out above, for emphasis we put some of the evidence of the defendant's agent here. He testified in part as follows: "I told him [the defendant] that Mr. Maxwell told me that he had signed a paper with Mr. Eaton. He knew about that when he got the sales contract signed." The evidence shows also that the defendant agreed to split the commission with Eaton.
It is our opinion that the evidence was sufficient to show that the defendant was not abiding strictly by the law under which he procured his broker's license and that the evidence was sufficient to warrant revocation of the license.
The trial judge committed reversible error in granting the defendant a new trial, under the entire record of this case.
Judgment reversed. Townsend and Carlisle, JJ., concur.
Paul S. Begin, pro se, contra.
Eugene Cook, Attorney-General, J. R. Parham, Assistant Attorney-General, for plaintiffs in error.
DECIDED JANUARY 22, 1958.
