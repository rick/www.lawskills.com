ROBERTS v. ROLAND.
36909.
(96 Ga. App. 673)
(101 SE2d 210)
(1957)
Money had and received. Before Judge Wright. Fulton Civil Court. August 6, 1957.
QUILLIAN, J.
Where to an action instituted by an administrator to recover money of his decedent wrongfully possessed by another, the defendant admits possession of the money and sets up the affirmative defense that under an agreement with the deceased she is entitled to retain a part of the money, the burden is on the defendant to adduce proof as to what part of the funds she is entitled. Where her evidence fails in this respect the trial judge should direct a verdict for the plaintiff.
Alex Roland, as administrator for the estate of Wilbert Roland, filed a suit against Alice Roberts. The petition alleged in part: Petitioner alleges that Alice Roberts, defendant, is indebted to the estate of Wilbert Roland, in the sum of $2,500, together with interest thereon, at the rate of 7% per annum from April 5, 1950, because of the facts hereinafter set out; that prior to and on April 5, 1950, Wilbert Roland had in his possession, or to his credit, in the United States Post Office, in Atlanta, Georgia, as postal savings, the sum of $2,500; that on April 5, 1950, Wilbert Roland was non compos mentis, and incompetent to manage his business affairs; petitioner alleges that on or about said date, defendant induced Wilbert Roland to draw out the $2,500 from savings and to deliver same to her, she well knowing at the time she received the money that Wilbert Roland was incompetent to manage his business affairs; that because of the fact of the deceased's incompetency at the time the money was delivered to the defendant, your petitioner alleges that the title to the money never passed to defendant, and that the money is a part of the estate of the deceased; that your petitioner, as guardian of the property of the deceased, and as his personal representative, is entitled to possession of the money, with interest thereon at the rate of 7% per annum, and to a judgment against the defendant for said sum with interest thereon, from the date she received the same and until the money is paid over to petitioner; that demand has been made upon the defendant for the money, and she refuses to deliver or pay it over to petitioner; that on April 5, 1950, Wilbert Roland withdrew a sum of money in excess of $1,090 from the postal savings and delivered the same to the defendant for the benefit of the deceased; that the sum of $1,090 was still in possession of the defendant on June 11, 1954, the date the deceased died; that following the death of Wilbert Roland the defendant converted the money and has failed and refused to deliver it to the plaintiff in his official capacity as guardian of the person and property of deceased and as administrator of the estate of Wilbert Roland.
The defendant filed an answer in which she denied the material allegations of the petition except that demand had been made upon her for the money in question.
On the trial the defendant testified in part: Wilbert Roland had lived in the house with her but she was not related to him; she received $1,090 from him; he told her to take the money and give him a decent burial; he wanted Hanley to bury him; she could have the money that was left over after paying Hanley for a decent burial; she did not pay for any of his burial expenses because she was unable to ascertain the amount due.
While the defendant in her answer did not admit receiving the money, she testified that the deceased gave the money to her. She further testified that she was entitled to retain that which was left after paying Hanley to provide a decent burial for the deceased. By this testimony she set up the affirmative defense that she was entitled to keep a part of the money. Having testified as to this affirmative defense the burden was on her to prove facts which would establish the defense. Jones v. Bank of Powder Springs, 31 Ga. App. 263 (120 S. E. 422); Cosmopolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Head, 23 Ga. App. 216 (98 S. E. 124); Williams v. Dennis, 38 Ga. App. 736 (145 S. E. 464); Higdon v. Williamson, 140 Ga. 187 (2) (78 S. E. 767); Wilson v. Wilson, 23 Ga. App. 94 (2) (97 S. E. 558). If the judge believed the defendant she would have been entitled to retain all money in excess of the price which Hanley charges for a decent burial. However, there was no evidence as to what it would have cost for Hanley to provide a decent burial; therefore, there was no method by which the judge could have determined the amount of money to which she was entitled. The defendant having failed to carry this burden the judge did not err in denying the motion for new trial.
Judgment affirmed. Felton, C. J., and Nichols, J., concur.
Brackett & Brackett, R. B. Pullen, contra.
Johnson, Hatcher & Meyerson, Henry M. Hatcher, Jr., Harvey A. Clein, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED NOVEMBER 13, 1957.
