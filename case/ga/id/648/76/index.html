THORNTON v. STATE HIGHWAY DEPARTMENT.
41812.
(113 Ga. App. 351)
(148 SE2d 66)
(1966)
Condemnation of land. Bibb Superior Court. Before Judge Long.
FELTON, Chief Judge.
1. The motion to dismiss the appeal on the ground that a judgment overruling a motion for a new trial is not an appealable judgment is denied.
2. Under the discovery provisions of the law (Ga. L. 1959, p. 425; Code Ann. Ch. 38-21) one party to a condemnation case may not by deposition or interrogatory ascertain from the other party the opinion of an independent expert appraiser employed and paid by the other party as to the value of the land being condemned and the value of the land remaining before and after taking.
This appeal from the judgment in a condemnation case involves three questions, the first two of which relate to pre-trial discovery questions which can be treated together. 1. Prior to the trial, appellant filed interrogatories seeking to obtain information showing the number of appraisers appellee had employed to render it, their appraisals of the property condemned, their identity, the date on which the appraisals were made, the opinions of value placed on the property condemned by each such appraiser, as well as such value opinions as to appellant's remaining property before and after condemnation. Appellee filed the following objections to said interrogatories and its motion to quash them upon the grounds stated in the objections. The grounds of the objections to the interrogatories are as follows: "(a) The information therein sought to be discovered is inadmissible upon the trial of this case and is not calculated to lead to the discovery of evidence which would be admissible upon the trial of this case; and (b) Condemnor should not be required to disclose the information sought by these interrogatories since it will be unfair to require the condemnor in this case to disclose the names of expert appraisers employed by it to render it opinions respecting the value of the land condemned; and (c) These interrogatories seek information which is by nature and character privileged, and as a matter of public policy condemnor should not be required to disclose such information; and (d) The matters referred to in these interrogatories do not constitute the kind of evidence that a party should be required to disclose to his adversary in this type of litigation. 2. Condemnor objects to condemnee's interrogatories Nos. 4, 5 and 6 on the ground that: (a) Condemnor should not be required to disclose the information called for by these three interrogatories, since it would be unfair to require condenmor in this case to disclose the opinions of experts employed by it to investigate the question of value of the property condemned, the value of the remaining property before condemnation and the value of the remaining property after condemnation, and (b) These interrogatories seek information which is by nature and character privileged, and as a matter of public policy condemnor should not be required to disclose such information; and (c) The matters referred to in these interrogatories do not constitute the kind of evidence that a party should be required to disclose to his adversary in this type of litigation. (d) Condemnor should not be required to disclose the information referred to in these interrogatories because it constitutes opinions formed by experts retained by condemnor to render such opinions to it." Upon hearing the objections the court sustained all of them and quashed the interrogatories. This is the subject matter of enumeration of error No. 1. 2. Prior to the trial, condemnee filed a motion requiring appellee to show cause why J. A. Leggett, a real estate appraiser, should not he compelled to submit to an examination and be required to answer questions propounded to him by appellant's counsel concerning his appraisal of the property condemned in this proceeding. This witness had previously refused to answer such questions upon the advice of counsel. The appellee and J. A. Leggett filed their verified response to said condemnee's motion in which it was shown, among other things, "that J. A. Leggett was employed by appellee as an expert real estate appraiser to make an appraisal and render to appellee, the condemnor in this proceeding, his expert opinion as to the amount of just compensation which should be paid for the land taken in this case and any damages which may have resulted to the owner's remaining property. Such response further shows that in making his said appraisal J. A. Leggett was not acting as an agent for appellee, but on the contrary was acting as an independent expert appraiser; that Leggett was paid a fee which was agreed upon between appellee and him for such appraisal; and that the appraisal and opinion which were submitted to appellee by the said J. A. Leggett was not approved by appellee as reflecting the amount of just compensation which should be paid for the taking of the property involved in this proceeding." Upon the hearing of such motion of condemnee J. A. Leggett to the court entered an order refusing to compel J. A. Leggett to divulge his expert opinion as to the amount of just compensation set forth in his appraisal report to the appellee. Such
refusal is the subject matter of enumeration of error No. 2. 3. On the trial of the case appellant called said J. A. Leggett as his witness who testified that more than two years previously he had made an appraisal of the property condemned at the request of the State Highway Department of Georgia; that he had forwarded his report of the appraisal to said department and that he had no notes concerning it; that he could not render an opinion as to the exact amount of his appraisal since he had no data or a copy of his report by means of which he could refresh his recollection. Appellant's counsel insisted that the witness testify as to the amount, in his opinion, of the value of the land, upon which insistence the court declined to require the witness to so testify, stating as a reason therefor, the reasons given by the witness for not being able to state anything but an approximate figure without having his report before him. Counsel for appellant stated for the record that if the witness was compelled to answer questions as to his appraised valuation of the property condemned, he expected said witness' answer to be $201,000. Counsel for the appellee stated that the case had been pending on appeal to a jury for about a year and that no notice to produce any appraisal report or subpoena duces tecum calling for production of such reports were ever served upon appellee in this case. No effort was made by appellant to obtain production of appraisal reports submitted by any expert appraisers to the Highway Department for use upon the trial of this case. The court's overruling of the request that the witness be compelled to answer the question propounded to him as to his appraisal of the property is the subject matter of enumerated error No. 3. The appellant made a motion for a new trial which was overruled and he appeals from the judgment overruling the motion for a new trial.
1. The motion to dismiss the appeal on the ground that the judgment overruling the motion for a new trial is not an appealable judgment under the Appellate Practice Act of 1965 (Ga. L. 1965, p. 18) is denied. Munday v. Brissette, 113 Ga. App. 147 (148 SE2d 55).
While it is true that in many civil cases under our federal rules it is requisite for the adversary to help a litigant on the other side of the case in the developing of his side of the case, it always has to do with the facts as observed by witnesses to an occurrence or to a transaction and is not applicable to matters of expert testimony. Lewis v. United Airlines, etc., D. C., 32 FSupp. 21. In fact where an expert employed by one of the parties to a cause of action has been requested to furnish his expert testimony to another, it is not only his privilege but his duty to refuse compensation from one of the parties where he has already accepted employment from the other and such refusal ought not of itself to result in his being ordered to testify. Boynton v. R. J. Reynolds Tobacco Co., D. C., 30 FSupp. 593, at page 595." In United States v. Sealy, supra, Judge W. A. Bootle of the United States District Court for the Middle District of Georgia, held that after an exhaustive study of the questions, he concluded that a majority of the Federal counts passing on this question had determined that the discovery sought in cases of this type should properly be denied. The record in this case does not reveal a showing by the applicant that the information he sought was necessary for his use in his preparation for the trial of the case, nor that he could not by his own independent investigation and research obtain an necessary expert opinion testimony concerning the value of his property. The applicant produced four expert real estate appraisers who testified as to the fair market value of the property. In United States v. 7,534.04 Acres of Land (N.D. Ga.) 18 F.R.D. 146, Judge Frank Hooper, District Judge for the Northern District of Georgia, ruled: "The land is open to inspection by all parties, no information concerning the same is sought from the Government that is not readily available to the defendants. The information sought therefore, is necessarily information obtained by the Government in preparation of the trial of the case and in the opinion of this court is not obtainable by interrogatories." The same rule would apply as to depositions. In Lewis v. United Airlines Transport Corp., (W.D. Pa.) 32 FSupp. 21, the court said. "To permit parties to examine the expert witnesses of the other party in land condemnation and patent actions, where the evidence nearly all comes from expert witnesses, would cause confusion and probably would violate that provision of Rule 1 of F. R. C. P., which provides that the rules 'shall be construed to secure the just, speedy, and inexpensive determination of every action.' " We conclude that as to the first two enumerations of error, the court did not commit error. The rulings in Atlantic C. L. R. Co. v. Daugherty, 111 Ga. App. 144 (141 SE2d 112), do not require a ruling contrary to those herein made. In the Daugherty case the court was concerned with discovery of statements taken by a claim agent or adjuster in the regular course of his employer's business. That case does not deal with statements or reports submitted by a party's independent expert real estate appraiser. In the Daugherty case this court stated: "Defendant contends that the matter sought by plaintiff's motion is privileged, or is the attorney's work product, but does not contend that it is not otherwise within the permissible scope." (Emphasis supplied.) It thus appears that the matter at issue in this case, whether the information sought is within the permissible scope of the discovery provision of the law, was not by any stretch of the imagination involved in the Daugherty case. None of the other cases cited by the appellant calls for a different ruling from that herein made.
Under the circumstances of this case, the estimate placed on the value of the land by Mr. Leggett would not be binding on either party or the jury, and it is our opinion that a jury would not be justified in accepting the appraisal of this particular witness based solely on the fact that he made the appraisal for one party to the case rather than for the other. In ruling upon this third enumeration of error, no decision or intimation is intended as to whether the communication was privileged or whether the witness could have been required to testify in the absence of any offer to compensate him for his services or to reimburse the Highway Department for its expenditure therefor. We only hold that the court did not err in refusing to require the witness to testify because he did not have the information at hand by which he could refresh his recollection.
The court did not err in overruling the motion for new trial. Judgment affirmed. Frankum and Pannell, JJ., concur.
