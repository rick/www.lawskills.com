GALLOWAY, by Next Friend, v. GREENWOOD et al.
36174.
(94 Ga. App. 145)
(94 SE2d 41)
(1956)
Tort; automobile wreck, pleadings. Before Judge Foster. Polk Superior Court. February 21, 1956.
QUILLIAN, J.
The trial judge erred in sustaining the general demurrer to the petition.
Gary Galloway, a minor, by Mrs. Flonnie Mae Cooper, as next friend, brought an action for damages against Charles T. Carter, Mrs. Ila Fred Carter and William Charles Greenwood. The case is here on exception to the trial judge's ruling sustaining a general demurrer as to the defendant Greenwood, and we shall discuss only the issue as to whether the petition set forth a cause of action against Greenwood, referred to herein after as the defendant.
The allegations of the petition in reference to the defendant Greenwood's negligence were in substance: That the plaintiff was a passenger in an automobile driven by Mrs. Carter; that Mrs. Carter was driving south on Highway 100 at speeds in excess of 70 miles an hour; that the plaintiff requested Mrs. Carter to slow down, which she failed to do; that the automobile approached a tractor-trailer truck proceeding in the same direction as the automobile driven by Mrs. Carter; that the tractor-trailer truck was being driven at a rate of speed not less than 55 miles per hour; that as Mrs. Carter approached the truck the defendant was driving an automobile behind her, traveling in the same direction; that Mrs. Carter and the defendant were driving not less than 75 miles per hour; that Mrs. Carter and the defendant passed the truck, Mrs. Carter still ahead of the defendant; that after passing said truck, Mrs. Carter failed to turn back to the right side of the highway and continued to drive on the left side of the highway at a speed not less than 75 miles per hour; that the defendant drove his automobile, negligently, to the right alongside the automobile operated by Mrs. Carter; that Mrs. Carter then swerved back to the right, preventing the defendant from passing her on the right; that the defendant then turned his automobile to the left and passed the automobile driven by Mrs. Carter; that as the defendant passed Mrs. Carter his automobile ran on the unpaved shoulder of the road on the left side of the highway; that the defendant's automobile began to swerve as it passed the automobile driven by Mrs. Carter and the car driven by her also began to swerve; that the defendant's automobile completely passed the automobile driven by Mrs. Carter; that as the result of the defendant's negligence and the negligence of Mrs. Carter, the automobile in which the plaintiff was riding veered across the highway and off the left side of the highway, turning over several times, and as a result the plaintiff received personal injuries. The petition alleged that the defendant was negligent in the following particulars: "In driving said 1949 Oldsmobile at a rate of speed greater than is reasonable and prudent under the conditions and having regard to the actual and potential hazards then existing; in operating said 1949 Oldsmobile at a speed greater than 60 miles an hour; in failing to reduce speed at a time when a special hazard existed with respect to other traffic; in attempting to pass on the right of the 1950 Oldsmobile so operated by defendant Mrs. Carter; in passing at the excessive speed herein described the automobile so operated by defendant Mrs. Carter."
The defendant Greenwood filed a general demurrer to the petition which the trial judge sustained without allowing any time to amend the petition. The plaintiff excepted to the order sustaining the general demurrer and also to the failure of the trial judge to allow the plaintiff to amend the petition.
1. The plaintiff insists that the trial judge erred in sustaining the general demurrer to the petition. In negligence cases, the petition, to set forth a cause of action, must show that the defendant failed to exercise the degree of care he owed the plaintiff and that his negligence proximately caused injury to the plaintiff or damage to his property. A petition lacking either of these elements is subject to general demurrer. Without question the averments as to the defendant Greenwood's conduct were sufficient to charge that on the occasion when the plaintiff was injured Greenwood failed to exercise ordinary care in the operation of his automobile, the degree of care required of him in the factual situation shown by the petition.
The question as to whether the petition showed causal connection between the defendant Greenwood's negligence and the event resulting in the plaintiff's injury is more difficult. If it did not appear from the facts alleged that Greenwood's negligence was the proximate cause of the injuries to the plaintiff, or if the facts pleaded revealed that some other intervening agency independent of the defendant's negligence was "the chief preponderating and proximate cause" of said injuries, the petition was properly dismissed on general demurrer. Shaw v. Mayor &c. of Macon, 6 Ga. App. 306 (64 S. E. 1102); Southern Ry. Co. v. Pair, 32 Ga. App. 378 (123 S. E. 142); Artope v. Central of Georgia R. Co., 38 Ga. App. 91 (143 S. E. 127).
But in Wright Contracting Co. v. Davis, 90 Ga. App. 548 (83 S. E. 2d 232), it was held, on page 552: "The plaintiff in error insists that the petition shows no causal connection between the defendant's negligence and the casualty suffered by the plaintiff. The facts pleaded show that the defendant was negligent in certain particulars that could have caused the death of the plaintiff's husband, and this is followed by the general allegation that these specified acts of negligence did have that proximate result. This was sufficient as against a general demurrer."
The petition does not very clearly show the specific manner in which the alleged negligence of the defendant Greenwood caused Mrs. Carter to lose control of her automobile. It is alleged: "As a result of the negligent operation of his automobile by the defendant Greenwood and as a result of the negligent operation by Mrs. Carter of the automobile of her husband, the defendant Mrs. Carter lost control of the automobile she was driving, said automobile swerved across the highway three or four times as she approached the railing of a bridge located on the highway, and then swerved to and off the left side of the highway after crossing said bridge."
However, it does contain the allegation that the defendant Greenwood's negligence, together with that of the defendant Mrs. Carter, caused the plaintiff to be injured. It cannot be said that the conduct of Greenwood in driving at a speed greater than was reasonable and safe when passing Mrs. Carter on her right, so that both automobiles involved swerved, did not materially contribute to the cause of Mrs. Carter's losing control of the automobile in which the plaintiff was riding when injured.
We think the petition set forth a cause of action. In view of what is here held, the question whether the trial judge should have allowed the plaintiff time in which to amend his petition becomes moot and will not be decided.
James I. Parker, Mundy & Gammon, contra.
Northcutt & Edwards, Edwin R. Johnston, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED JULY 9, 1956.
