WILLIAMS v. WOFFORD, Building Inspector, et al.
22726.
(220 Ga. 504)
(140 SE2d 190)
(1965)
Injunction. Fulton Superior Court. Before Judge McKenzie.
MOBLEY, Justice.
1. Where plans and application for a building permit were submitted under the 1954 City of Atlanta Zoning Ordinance and the permit issued the day following adoption of the 1963 City Zoning Ordinance, such permit was legally issued within the meaning of the 1963 ordinance, which contained a six months waiver period during which those owning property before approval of the 1963 ordinance could build according to regulations in effect prior to its approval.
2. Defendant began construction and use of its property within six months from approval of the 1963 ordinance, as required by this ordinance, when it began demolishing and removing a dwelling on the property, grading the property, and excavating and pouring footings for the apartment building.
On or about June 1, 1963, application was made by Summer Hill Apartments, Inc., for a building permit to erect an apartment house on a tract of land in the City of Atlanta which was situated in a district zoned for apartment house use under the 1954 city zoning ordinance, and plans were submitted based on the 1954 ordinance. On June 19, 1963, an ordinance was adopted by the city which amended many of the regulations relating to the construction of apartment houses. The following day, June 20, a building permit was issued to Summer Hill Apartments, Inc., the defendant, who started construction work in the following December. Objection was made by one J. H. Moore, a representative of the Ansley Park Civic Club, that defendant was violating a restrictive covenant relating to set back distance, and new plans were submitted to comply with this restriction; thereafter, objection was made that the revised plans did not conform to certain ordinance requirements, and they were revised accordingly.
Plaintiff, an adjacent land owner, brought her petition seeking temporary and permanent injunction against W. R. Wofford, Building Inspector, to prevent his issuing a building permit to Summer Hill Apartments, Inc., and against Summer Hill's erecting an apartment building on grounds that the latter's plans violated the 1954 and 1963 ordinances; that defendant, Summer Hill Apartments, Inc., lacked a valid or legally issued building permit, and that defendant had failed to begin construction within the meaning of the amended ordinance of June 19, 1963. A temporary restraining order issued, and on July 30, 1964, after hearing, the court issued its order finding that the building permit had been lawfully issued, and that defendant began construction and use of the property within six months from June 19, 1963, as required by the terms of the 1963 ordinance. Thereupon, the court revoked the temporary restraining order and denied plaintiff's prayer for injunction, and plaintiff excepts to this judgment.
Therefore, we conclude that the permit was lawfully issued.
2. Did defendant forfeit its right to proceed under the 1954 ordinance by failing to begin construction and use of its property within six months after the adoption of the 1963 ordinance? The answer to this question must be negative.
Defendant began construction and use of the property under its building permit when on or about December 4, 1963, it started work by demolishing and removing a two story dwelling located on the property, by grading of the property, and by excavating for the footings of the building. Some of these footings were poured early in December and were inspected and approved by the building inspector on December 12, and December 17, 1963. All the aforementioned acts took place within a six month period from the adoption of the June 19, 1963, ordinance. Plaintiff, however, claims that this work was not beginning construction within the six month period because the footings were covered up in April and new ones poured. The facts underlying this act indicate that defendant began use and construction in good faith within the six month period and covered up its original footings poured during this period and poured new ones only because a restriction on the property requiring all buildings to be set back at least 125 feet from the street was called to defendant's attention, and it changed the footings only to meet this restriction of which it had no former knowledge. We, therefore, conclude that defendant in good faith began construction and use of its property within the required six month period.
Accordingly, since defendant was lawfully issued a building permit and began construction within the required six months, the judgment of the trial court is affirmed.
Judgment affirmed. All the Justices concur.
Grant, Spears & Duckworth, Henry L. Bowden, Robert F. Lyle, Edwin L. Sterne, contra.
Mitchell, Clarke, Pate & Anderson, Stephens Mitchell, for plaintiff in error.
