CORN v. CENTRAL TRUCK LINES, INC., et al.
37248.
(98 Ga. App. 28)
(104 SE2d 662)
(1958)
Action for damages; nonsuit. Fulton Superior Court. Before Judge Wood. April 23, 1958.
GARDNER, Presiding Judge.
The trial court did not err in granting the motion of the defendant Central Truck Lines, Inc., for a nonsuit.
Mrs. J. L. Corn brought suit against Central Truck Lines, Inc., and Sam B. Jenkins, Jr. The petition alleged negligence on the part of the defendants, such negligence causing the death of the plaintiff's husband. The deceased was a guest passenger in a station wagon operated by the defendant Jenkins, which station wagon was involved in a collision with a tractor-trailer of the defendant Central Truck Lines at approximately 9:45 p.m. on June 7, 1956.
Mr. Hollis Entrekin testified that he also was a passenger in the Nash Rambler car belonging to Sam Jenkins, Jr., on a trip through Central Georgia and that Jenkins did all of the driving; that they stopped in Barnesville, Georgia, to have a beer buying an extra one each to drink in the car; that they each consumed two beers; that they stopped in McRae for gas and a soft drink; that they had the seats in the Nash Rambler made into beds except the driver's seat and everyone except the driver was in a reclining position; that Mr. Jenkins was driving about 50 to 60 miles per hour and that just below McRae on Highway 341 they were involved in an accident. On cross-examination the same witness testified that neither he nor any of the other passengers had complained of Mr. Jenkins's driving.
William Pace testified approximately the same as the witness Entrekin and identified pictures of the wreck and the scene of the accident, and testified that the Jenkins car collided with something and turned over several times; that he never did see the truck that was supposed to be involved; that he was injured and was in no condition to make any investigation or examination of the wreck; and that a Ford came along afterwards and struck the back end of the station wagon.
Marvis Holcombe, the driver of the 1951 White truck owned by the defendant Central Truck Lines, Inc., which truck was involved in the accident, testified that he first observed the approaching car when it was about 500 or 600 feet from him; that he had dimmed his lights; that he observed the approaching car coming straight down the center line and astride it and he blinked his headlights in an effort to draw the driver's attention and get him over on his side of the road; that he estimated the speed of the approaching car at about 80 miles per hour; that the car never did change its course and he cut his truck to the right trying to give room but the station wagon collided with his truck and he then traveled about 400 feet before coming to a stop.
Sam B. Jenkins, the driver of the Nash automobile, testified that he drove the car at 50 miles per hour; that he did not at any time get across the center line; that he didn't notice anything unusual about the lights on the approaching truck; that "they were on his side of the road and so were mine and nothing was out of the ordinary, until we got just even with this, I didn't even know it was a truck, just a pair of lights meeting like so many others did that night. When we had gotten there, there was a noise and the next thing I knew it was the next day."
Counsel for the plaintiff cite Elrod v. McConnell, 170 Ga. 892, 893 (154 S. E. 449) to the effect that a trial judge always grants nonsuits at his peril and that if there is any evidence upon which a verdict could be rendered, the case should be submitted to a jury. This is a true principle of law. To the same effect see Wright v. Roseman, 209 Ga. 176 (71 S. E. 2d 426), Ellison v. Evans, 85 Ga. App. 292 (69 S. E. 2d 94), Keebler v. Willard, 86 Ga. App. 884 (72 S. E. 2d 805), and Gray v. Schlapp, 92 Ga. App. 261 (88 S. E. 2d 536). There are many other decisions to this same effect. It is also true that, where there is no proof to support the plaintiff's allegations, a nonsuit should be granted. See Vickers v. Atlanta & W. P. R. Co., 64 Ga. 306, 307, and Corcoran v. Merchants & Miners Transportation Co., 1 Ga. App. 741, 743 (57 S. E. 962). There are many other decisions to this same effect. The appellate courts have many times held that negligence, whose negligence and what negligence are questions for the determination of a jury. However, each case where the granting of a nonsuit is involved must be determined under the facts of the particular case involved. In determining questions as to whether or not the nonsuit was properly granted it was our thought to go extensively into the many citations regarding the granting of nonsuits. It was also our thought to cite from Whited v. Atlanta Coca-Cola Bottling Co., 88 Ga. App. 241 (76 S. E. 2d 408) division 2, wherein this court went extensively into the question now before us. The facts of that case are not the facts as now before us but while studying that case it would seem that all of division 2 with reference to the granting of nonsuits is applicable to the case now under consideration so far as the principles of law are concerned, and this court cited many, many decisions involving nonsuits in that case. We are tempted to quote extensively from that case but rather than encumber the Court of Appeals Reports with the repetition of the matter there contained, we merely invite the bench and bar to read what is said in that case.
In the instant case, as in all nonsuit cases, the question is: Was the evidence on behalf of the plaintiff sufficient to prove the material allegations of the petition? The evidence in the case before us is not sufficient to show that the defendant Central's trailer-tractor was at fault, nor was there any evidence upon which to hang a reversal of the judgment of the trial judge granting a nonsuit. The only positive testimony was in favor of the defendant Central. The driver of the station wagon testified substantially that he did not know what occurred. We can see nothing to support the inference that the defendant Central was responsible for the collision and for this reason we cannot conscientiously have this case submitted to a jury. We are always most willing for a jury to determine questions of fact but where, as here, there is nothing to warrant a case being submitted to a jury for determination of facts, we must recognize the legal technique of sustaining the granting of a nonsuit.
Since there is a fatal variance between the allegations of the petition and the proof, we hold that the trial judge did not err in his ruling.
Judgment affirmed. Townsend and Carlisle, JJ., concur.
T. J. Long, Ben Weinberg, Jr., contra.
Harry P. Hall, Jr., John L. Westmoreland, John L. Westmoreland, Jr., for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED JULY 16, 1958.
