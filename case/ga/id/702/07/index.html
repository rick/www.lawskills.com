HORNE et al. v. PHILLIPS.
35808.
(92 Ga. App. 651)
(89 SE2d 682)
(1955)
Complaint. Before Judge Averill. Oglethorpe City Court. May 13, 1955.
GARDNER, P. J.
The court committed no reversible error in the grant of a first new trial.
This case is more difficult to determine than to state. It is easy to state because there is only one question involved. This arises under the provisions of Code 6-1608, which reads: "The first grant of a new trial shall not be disturbed by the appellate court, unless the plaintiff in error shall show that the judge abused his discretion in granting it, and the law and facts required the verdict notwithstanding the judgment of the presiding judge."
Howard W. Horne, Jr., Willie Hugh Horne, by H. W. Horne, Sr., as next friend, will hereinafter be referred to as the plaintiffs, and Kelly Phillips will be hereinafter referred to as the defendant. The defendant obtained a judgment against H. W. Horne, Sr., and levied upon certain property. Thereupon, H. W Horne, Sr., as next friend, filed a claim to property levied on under the fi. fa. of the defendant on behalf of his two minor boys, Howard W. Horne, Jr., 15 years of age, and Willie Hugh Horne, 16 years of age. The question was submitted to a jury, and a verdict was rendered for the plaintiffs. The defendant filed a motion for new trial, which the court granted. The plaintiffs filed exceptions to this judgment and brought the case here for review.
Briefly, the evidence reveals that H. W. Horne, Sr., rented a farm from Carey H. Phillips, a brother of the defendant, in the name of H. W Horne, Sr., giving a rent note in his own name to cover the rent. He went to the store of the defendant, and made arrangements for credit with the defendant for the year 1954, the year the farm was used. At this time, H. W. Horne, Sr., informed the defendant that he, H. W. Horne, Sr., had rented a nearby farm and desired to open an account with the defendant; and that he was receiving a pension and could pay the account monthly. At this time no mention was made that the minor boys would operate the farm. The manager of the A.A.A. and S.E.A. office testified that the cotton and peanut marketing cards were issued by his office in the name of H. W. Horne, Sr., and that the Government officer stated that nothing was ever said to the effect that H. W. Horne, Sr., was the agent for his boys. Another witness testified that he sold a mule to H. W. Horne, Sr. Mrs. Horne brought the money to pay for the mule. The witness had never heard of the two minor boys operating the farm. He made the trade with H. W. Horne, Sr.
Another witness testified that he operated a warehouse in Oglethorpe; that he purchased six bales of cotton from H. W. Horne, Sr., and gave a check covering the cotton to H. W. Horne, Sr.; that nothing was said about the minor boys operating the farm or having anything to do with the cotton. The witness never heard of the minor boys having operated a farm in 1954.
The defendant testified in his own behalf that he was the plaintiff in fi. fa., and he operated a store close to the farm which H. W. Horne, Sr., rented from the defendant's brother, Carey, for the year 1954; that H. W. Horne, Sr., came to the defendant's store and told the defendant that he had rented the farm of the defendant's brother and wanted the defendant to open an account for H. W. Horne, Sr., stating that he would pay each month, that H. W. Horne, Sr., did not do this, and the defendant obtained a judgment against H. W. Horne, Sr., and levied upon the property in question; that the defendant had never heard that the two minor boys were operating the farm; that H. W. Horne, Sr., told the defendant that he was receiving a Government pension, and that, if H. W. Horne, Sr., had told the defendant that the minor boys were operating the farm, the defendant would not have extended credit.
The evidence further revealed that H. W. Horne, Sr., along with his wife and two minor sons, lived on the farm, all of them working on or about the farm, as is the custom for a farming family. The boys went to school. Previously to 1954, the older boy was involved in an accident while riding in a school bus and received $2,000 net in settlement. The father qualified as guardian. The plaintiffs contend that the farm was operated by the boys, and that a part of the $2,000 above mentioned was used in farm operations. The purchases by the plaintiffs from the defendant's store were used by the family during the farming operations. There was no evidence as to how much of the $2,000 was used in the operation of the farm. The only evidence to this effect was that "some" of the $2,000 was used. H. W. Horne, Sr., testified that he did not know how much of the boy's money he had on hand. The testimony revealed further that the boys had received only $25 from their father for the farm produce sold by the father. This is not all the evidence, but we think it is sufficient to give an overall picture of the whole transaction.
1. The first proposition of law is that the property levied upon was found in the possession of H. W. Horne, Sr. This being true, the burden of proof was on the plaintiffs to prove that the property levied upon was not subject to fi. fa. We call attention to Crawford v. Kimbrough, 76 Ga. 299. See also Smith v. Wellborn, 75 Ga. 799.
2. The evidence, under all the facts and circumstances of this case, did not demand a verdict in favor of the plaintiffs. Under the evidence the judge was authorized to grant the first new trial. He was authorized to grant this trial for the first time to review his charge, in view of all the evidence. Of course, this charge is not before this court, but the trial court might consider it in view of all the facts and circumstances in order to determine whether or not the verdict is contrary to the evidence and to determine whether or not the verdict is contrary to law and the principles of justice and equity. The court might desire, also, to consider whether or not the arrangements of the plaintiffs were free from fraud because of the secret operation and conduct of the plaintiffs. These are questions which this court feels that the trial court should have the authority to investigate on a second trial. The trial court did not abuse its discretion in the giant of a first net trial.
Jared J. Bull, contra.
Troy G. Morrow, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED SEPTEMBER 30, 1955.
