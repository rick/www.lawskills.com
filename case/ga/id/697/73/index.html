KING v. MANUFACTURERS ACCEPTANCE CORP.
36471.
(94 Ga. App. 899)
(96 SE2d 522)
(1957)
Action on contract. Before Judge Parker. Fulton Civil Court. September 13, 1956.
TOWNSEND, J.
1. The assignment of error in the bill of exceptions on the overruling of certain demurrers, not being argued or insisted upon, is treated as abandoned.
2. The only remaining assignment of error is the denial of the motion for new trial on the general grounds. No error is assigned on the charge of the court, and the charge is not included in the record. Accordingly, this court cannot consider the argument in the brief of counsel for the plaintiff in error that the trial court erred in submitting to the jury the construction of the contract between the plaintiff and the defendant relative to the disposition of the reserve fund held by the former. Code 6-901.
3. The evidence authorizes a finding that the defendant was indebted to the plaintiff as alleged in the petition, and against his plea of conversion and avoidance to the effect that plaintiff was retaining other funds of the defendant in excess of this amount. Under the evidence, the jury was authorized to conclude that the plaintiff had a right at all times to retain in its reserve account funds up to 7% of outstanding retail accounts as a protection against contingent losses on time payment contracts of purchasers of automobiles, which contracts were purchased by the plaintiff from the defendant without recourse, and that it was entitled to continue to hold the reserve on hand as against contingent losses instead of applying it on the payment of the debt forming the basis for this action. Accordingly, the general grounds of the motion for a new trial are without merit.
The trial court did not err in denying the motion for new trial.
Manufacturers Acceptance Corporation filed suit in the Civil Court of Fulton County against the plaintiff in error trading as King Motors for the sum of $731.96, being the balance owing under a note, trust receipt and bill of sale on an automobile sold by the defendant dealer after having been "floor planned" to the plaintiff finance company. The defendant contended in his ansWer that during the course of the floor plan agreement the plaintiff had built up a reserve fund in the sum of $745.25, that this sum belonged to the defendant, that the defendant had called upon the plaintiff to deduct the amount owing from this reserve fund, and that the plaintiff refused to do so, for which reason he was not indebted to plaintiff but was entitled to have the debt canceled and the balance of $14.25 refunded to him.
Upon the trial of the case, the evidence showed an agreement between the plaintiff and the defendant whereby the finance company took title to and lent the dealer money on the automobiles in his lot. Upon sale of an automobile with time payments, the finance company purchased the conditional bills of sale from the dealer at a discount price without recourse, placing a designated sum realized out of the customer's time payments into a reserve fund, the purpose of which was to pay off losses on repossessions. The contract recites the following: "We shall set up $30 reserve on all 1949 and older models. On 1950 and later models where the advance is $1,000 or more, for 24 months, we shall set up $50 reserve. We shall hold all the reserve for losses on all 1949 and older models. We will pay 50% of the reserve on all 1950's and later models monthly. The reserve we hold for losses is to be held until the reserve equals 7% of the total retain outstanding. The reserve held for losses, as finally determined by us, means all losses on any particular deal goes against the total reserve for losses."
The amount put into the reserve fund held by the finance company comes from funds added to the contract of sale with the retail customer in the form of carrying charges, and is in addition to the agreed selling price. It is not collected in cases where the full cash price for the automobile is paid within 30 days. Otherwise, according to the testimony of the plaintiff's manager, it is paid into the reserve account until the total reserve account reaches 7% of the outstanding conditional-sale contract notes, and the excess, if any, is paid over to the dealer as additional profit, the finance company at all times until final liquidation of all accounts retaining 7% in its fund. According to the uncontradicted evidence in the case this is a universal trade custom followed by all finance companies under similar contracts for floor planning arrangements.
On September 28, 1955, the defendant sold an automobile and sent the plaintiff his check for $731.96 in satisfaction of the loan outstanding thereon, which check was returned for insufficient funds. On October 14, the defendant notified the plaintiff to deduct this amount from the reserve account, which on that date amounted to $745.25, with outstanding retail accounts of $9,118.63. This the plaintiff refused to do on the ground that it would reduce the reserve account below the 7% limit. At the date of trial the reserve account had been further reduced to $307.25 by reason of losses on repossessions, with retail accounts still outstanding of $1,770.70 of which $91 was known to be uncollectible. Under the testimony, the reserve fund was for the sole purpose of protecting the plaintiff against losses on time payment contracts assigned to it, and was not for the purpose of reimbursing the plaintiff for loans made by it to the defendant.
The jury returned a verdict for the plaintiff.
Fraser & Shelfer, contra.
Long & Jones, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED JANUARY 22, 1957.
