PARKS v. STEIN STEEL & SUPPLY COMPANY.
33877.
(85 Ga. App. 306)
(68 SE2d 919)
(1952)
Action for damages; from Fulton Civil Court-- Judge Parker. October 9, 1951.
GARDNER, J.
1. The writ of error in the instant case is not subject to dismissal because the other defendant in the petition was not made a party to the bill of exceptions, was not served, and did not waive service. This is true because such other defendant did not demur to the petition, and is, therefore, not interested in or affected by the judgment of the court below, as explained in the opinion.
2. The petition not affirmatively showing on its face that the proximate cause of the damage to the plaintiff's automobile was the negligence of the defendant Reeves, it does not affirmatively appear as a matter of law that no cause of action as to the corporate defendant was set out.
Arlee Parks filed suit in the Civil Court of Fulton County against Stein Steel & Supply Company and J. H. Reeves, seeking to recover for damage to his automobile caused by the alleged negligence of the defendants. The petition alleged substantially these facts: During the early part of April, 1951, the plaintiff was driving his 1949 Plymouth automobile in a westerly direction along Decatur Street, a public thoroughfare in the City of Atlanta, in said State and county; that, at a point just east of the intersection of Butler and Decatur Streets, a certain 1950 Mack truck, belonging to said corporate defendant "ran head on into the front end of plaintiff's automobile"; that Decatur Street, at said point of collision was of sufficient width to accommodate two lanes of vehicular traffic in both directions (however, parking was allowed on both sides of Decatur Street); that at said time the plaintiff was driving on his right-hand side of the middle of Decatur Street, and automobiles were parked on such street, facing west, to his right and against the north curb thereof; that at said time the plaintiff was driving his car in a careful manner and at a lawful speed not exceeding 15 miles an hour; that, just prior to the collision, the Mack truck was being operated in an easterly direction on Decatur Street; that, upon reaching a point just before where the said truck would ordinarily pass the plaintiff's automobile, the driver of the truck suddenly and without any warning turned the truck sharply to his left, causing the truck to suddenly veer to the driver's left and onto his left side of the center of Decatur Street and into the front end of the plaintiff's automobile, damaging the same; that, just before the said driver of the truck veered the truck to his left, the defendant Reeves, driving a 1949 Chevrolet automobile, which had been parked against the south curb of Decatur Street, facing east, started his car and caused the same to move forward and the front end outward into Decatur Street immediately into the path of the oncoming Mack truck of the corporate defendant; that, when the driver of the truck veered the same to his left, the plaintiff was unable to avoid being struck and could not suddenly swerve his car to his right, due to the fact that cars were parked along Decatur Street immediately to his right; that the truck was then and there being operated by the driver, William Render, in connection with the business of the corporation and within the scope of his authority as a truck driver for such defendant; that at such time there was in force and effect a valid ordinance of said City of Atlanta, known as Atlanta City Code, sec. 88-916, reading that, "Upon all roadways of sufficient width a vehicle shall be driven upon the right half of the roadway"; and said corporate defendant in violating the provisions of such ordinance was negligent per se; that said corporate defendant was further negligent in failing to warn the plaintiff that it was about to veer the truck to its left, and in failing to apply the brakes thereof before striking the front end of the plaintiff's car, and. in driving the front end of the truck into the front end of the plaintiff's car; that the defendant Reeves was negligent in driving his Chevrolet car suddenly out from the south curb of Decatur Street without warning, and into Decatur Street and the south middle traffic lane thereof immediately in
the path of the oncoming truck of said corporate defendant; that the impact of the truck against the plaintiff's car catapulted the same into and against another car parked against the north curb of Decatur Street with great force and impact; and that the market value of the plaintiff's automobile has been diminished $695, for which he asks judgment of the defendants.
The defendant, Stein Steel & Supply Company, demurred generally to the petition, on the ground that the same neither in whole nor in part sets out a cause of action against this defendant, and for the reason that the petition affirmatively shows on its face that the proximate cause of said injury was the acts of negligence of the defendant J. H. Reeves.
The trial judge, Hon. Wilson Parker, sustained the demurrer of Stein Steel & Supply Company on the ground that "no cause of action is set out against the defendant, Stein Steel & Supply Company, and that the petition clearly shows that the proximate cause of the plaintiff's injury was the act of negligence of the other defendant," J. H. Reeves, and said general demurrer is hereby sustained "and the petition as to Stein Steel & Supply Company . . . is hereby dismissed." The defendant Reeves did not demur.
From the foregoing judgment the plaintiff sued out a writ of error to this court, making the defendant, Stein Steel & Supply Company, the defendant in error therein, but not making the defendant, J. H. Reeves, a party defendant in error to said bill of exceptions. Said Reeves was not served with a copy of the bill of exceptions nor did he waive service. A motion is made in this court to dismiss the bill of exceptions because all parties interested in sustaining the judgment of the trial court complained of are not joined in the bill of exceptions.
1. While it is true that "All persons who are interested in sustaining or reversing the judgment of the court below are indispensable parties in the Supreme Court and Court of Appeals, and they must be made parties to the bill of exceptions" ( Edwards v. Wall, 153 Ga. 776, 113 S. E. 190), this rule is on the theory that they are interested in sustaining or reversing the judgment excepted to; but where, as here, there is a suit against two defendants, charging separate acts of negligence as to each, and one of the defendants demurs on the ground that it appears affirmatively from the petition that the plaintiff's damage was the result of the negligence of the other defendant and not of the demurrant, and the trial court sustains this demurrer on that ground and dismisses the case as to that defendant, and the case remains in court as to the other defendant who did not demur, and where the plaintiff excepts directly to the judgment sustaining said demurrer and in the bill of exceptions to this court the defendant who did not demur and against whom the case was not dismissed, but is yet pending in the trial court, was not made a party defendant in error, such defendant is not an indispensable party defendant in error in this court, not being interested in sustaining or reversing the judgment of the trial court. The failure of the plaintiff in error to make him a party defendant in error will not authorize a dismissal of the bill of exceptions. Edwards v. Wall, 153 Ga. 776, supra; Teasley v. Cordell, 153 Ga. 397 (112 S. E. 287); Anderson v. Haas, 160 Ga. 420 (128 S. E. 178).
There is nothing in the case of Tillman v. Davis, 147 Ga. 206 (93 S. E. 201), to the contrary of what we now rule. That case was an action against several defendants, and a general demurrer was interposed by some of them, which demurrer went to the substance of the whole petition and challenged the plaintiff's right to any relief, thus enuring to the benefit of all the defendants. The demurrer was sustained and the petition dismissed as to all the defendants. The plaintiff, complaining of the judgment sustaining the demurrer, sued out a writ of error to the Supreme Court, but he did not make the non-demurring defendants parties thereto, nor were they served with the bill of exceptions, nor did they acknowledge and waive service thereof. Those non-demurring defendants in the Tillman case were interested in retaining the judgment of the trial court in its existing status. With that judgment extant there was no case in the trial court in favor of the plaintiff against any of the defendants. The Supreme Court there held: "Where the judge sustains such demurrer and enters an order dismissing the whole case, such dismissal enures to the benefit of all the defendants, and they become interested in sustaining the judgment; and if the plaintiff desires to except to the ruling, it is essential that he make all of the defendants in the trial court defendants in error and serve them wish a copy of the bill of exceptions, and a failure to do so will require a dismissal of the writ of error." Tillman v. Davis, supra, citing U. S. Leather Co. v. First National Bank, 107 Ga. 263 (33 S. E. 31); Davis v. Walters, 140 Ga. 229 (78 S. E. 838). The Tillman case is entirely different from the case at bar. That difference is in a very vital and material aspect. The non-demurring defendant here was not necessarily interested in sustaining the judgment of the trial court, whereas in the Tillman case they were directly interested in sustaining such judgment. The petition had not been dismissed as to him, but there was pending in the trial court an action for damages by the plaintiff against him. The demurrant (Stein Steel & Supply Company) in the trial court had attacked the plaintiff's petition as not setting forth a cause of action against it, for the reason that it affirmatively appeared from the face of the petition that the proximate cause of the plaintiff's injuries was the acts of negligence of the defendant Reeves. The trial judge in sustaining this demurrer ruled: "This court is of the opinion that no cause of action is set out against the defendant Stein Steel & Supply Company, add that the petition clearly shows that the proximate cause of plaintiff's injuries was the act of negligence of the other defendant" Reeves, and the demurrer "is hereby sustained and the petition as to the Stein Steel & Supply Company . . . is hereby dismissed."
It follows that the motion of the defendant in error to dismiss this bill of exceptions is without merit and the same is hereby denied.
2. We come next to consider whether the allegations of the defendant in error here as to its negligence in the premises presented a jury question regarding the allegations of negligence. The plaintiff charges that the driver of the corporate defendant's truck was operating the same on Decatur Street, meeting the plaintiff who was likewise driving his automobile along Decatur Street in the opposite direction; and that there were automobiles parked along both sides of Decatur Street at this point, and there was barely enough width for two lanes of vehicular traffic, and when the truck reached a point where it would ordinarily pass by the plaintiff's car proceeding in the opposite direction, the driver of the truck suddenly, and without any warning to the plaintiff, turned the truck sharply to his left and directly into the plaintiff's automobile, causing the same to be thrown against another car and to be damaged as alleged. There is an ordinance of the City of Atlanta (Code, City of Atlanta, see. 88-916), which is set up by the plaintiff, providing that, "Upon all roadways of sufficient width a vehicle shall be driven upon the right half of the roadway"; and it is charged that the driver of the truck did not so drive the truck in accordance with the provisions of this ordinance, and that the corporate defendant was thereby negligent per se. The petition set up that this defendant corporation was also negligent, in that the driver of the truck failed to warn the plaintiff that he was about to suddenly turn to his left and in front of the plaintiff, and in failing to apply his brakes before striking the plaintiff's car. It is true that the petition also alleges that the defendant Reeves suddenly drove out directly in front of the oncoming truck and in the same traffic lane, which was a question of negligence. Questions of negligence, proximate cause, and what negligence, as well as whose negligence, caused the injury, are for the jury and not law questions for the court, except in plain and unmistakable cases. See Speed Oil Co. of Calhoun v. Jones, 59 Ga. App. 625, 627 (1 S. E. 2d, 760); Tybee Amusement Co. v. Odum, 51 Ga. App. 1 (3) (179 S. E. 415). The petition does not show, as a matter of law, that the proximate cause of the injury to the plaintiff's automobile was the alleged negligence of the defendant Reeves in driving his car out from the curb without warning, directly in front of the truck, thereby causing the truck to turn to the left to avoid striking the same and to strike the plaintiff's car. A jury may be authorized to find, under the evidence, that the defendant corporation was negligent as alleged. The case against the corporation as alleged in the petition is not such a plain and indisputable one as can be solved by the court as a matter of law on demurrer; but the petition raises questions for determination by the jury under proper instructions from the court. See Bozeman v. Blue's Truck Line, 62 Ga. App. 7 (7 S. E. 2d, 412); Tallman v. Green, 74 Ga. App. 731 (41 S. E. 2d, 339). It cannot be held as a matter of law that the sole proximate
cause of the damage to the car of the plaintiff was the act of Reeves in driving his car out from the curb in front of the truck.
Applying the foregoing rulings, the petition did not affirmatively show that the proximate cause of the damage to the automobile of the plaintiff was the negligent acts of the defendant Reeves, and the trial court erred in so ruling and in dismissing the petition as to the defendant, Stein Steel & Supply Company.
Judgment reversed. MacIntyre, P. J., and Townsend, J., concur.
Fine & Efurd, for defendant.
George T. Manley, Frank A. Bowers, for plaintiff.
DECIDED FEBRUARY 1, 1952.
