SHEPARD v. GETTYS.
16895.
(206 Ga. 392)
(57 SE2d 272)
(1950)
DUCKWORTH, Chief Justice.
When a petition for specific performance alleges that the purchase price of $4500 was payable in cash after the title had been approved, that it was subsequently found that a timber lease was outstanding, that the parties then mutually agreed that the purchase-money should be paid at the expiration of the lease, and that when the lease expired the seller informed the purchaser of his intention to repudiate the contract of sale, this would constitute a waiver of tender. The petition is not subject to general demurrer upon the ground that the petitioner had not included in his tender of the principal amount any interest thereon.
Specific performance. Before Judge Rowland. Laurens Superior Court. September 15, 1949.
J. M. Gettys by his petition alleged: that Shepard, the defendant, was seeking by a distress warrant to oust the plaintiff from possession of certain described real property on the claim that the plaintiff was a tenant of the defendant; that the plaintiff was not and never was the tenant of the defendant; that he held the premises as owner, under a contract of purchase made between himself and Shepard; that in the purchase of said property he agreed to pay $4500 in cash, the property to be unencumbered, with "good and merchantable title in fee simple, subject to the approval of your petitioner's attorney," and upon the approval of the title to pay that sum in cash. He further alleged: that, upon an examination of the title, it was discovered that the lands were encumbered by a timber lease which would expire on August 15, 1948; that the defendant agreed to postpone payment of the purchase-money until after that date to determine the timber loss; that the defendant then and there ratified and confirmed the sale of the land and placed the plaintiff in possession; that the plaintiff entered into possession and has erected valuable improvements there on; that on August 15, 1948, the timber lease expired without any of the timber having been cut; that immediately thereafter the plaintiff contacted the defendant for the purpose of paying the purchase-money and closing the sale of the land; that the defendant refused to comply with the contract, demanded surrender of the possession of the land and $300 as rental thereon; that the plaintiff has tendered the $4500, continued to tender the same, and now tenders that amount into court. There was the further allegation that the purchase-price is wholly adequate and more than the true market value of the land, and the defendant has no reason or excuse, in equity or good conscience for repudiating the contract.
The plaintiff prayed for a decree of specific performance of the contract, title to the land, and that the defendant be restrained and enjoined from further prosecution of the distress warrant. General and special demurrers were filed, heard, and overruled. The case is here for review of that judgment.
(After stating the foregoing facts.) Counsel for the plaintiff in error contends that the general demurrer should have been sustained because there was no allegation of tender of any accrued interest. The third headnote of Parke v. Foster, 26 Ga. 465, is relied upon for support of this contention. It is there held that "A cash sale of property bears interest from date, although the day of payment be postponed until a particular event transpired." It is insisted that, since the contract in the instant case provided that the consideration shall be paid in cash, it was a cash sale, and the subsequent mutual agreement to postpone payment until the expiration of the timber lease was merely a postponement of payment "until a particular event transpired," thus bringing it under the rule stated in the Parke case.
While it is true that the present contract provides a consideration of $4500 in cash, the petitioner alleges that the contract provided that this consideration must be paid "immediately upon the approval of the title to said lands by his attorney." Yet it is further alleged that the examination of the title disclosed an encumbrance of the title in the form of a timber lease, and the parties thereupon mutually agreed that at the expiration of the lease the purchase-price would be reduced in amount equal to the value of any timber that had been cut, and the petitioner would pay the remainder of the purchase-price at that time. In the absence of an express agreement, this purchase-money would not draw interest until it became due, and since, by mutual agreement, it was not due until the timber lease expired, and there being no agreement to pay interest before maturity, the full amount of purchase-money due was the principal of $4500.
Counsel for the plaintiff in error further relies upon the decision in Lively v. Munday, 201 Ga. 409 (40 S. E. 2d, 62, 173 A. L. R. 1295), wherein it was held that the purchasers were liable for interest on the purchase-price from the time they took possession. That decision does not hold that the mere act of taking possession created a liability for interest on the purchase-money. It is there pointed out that the contract made it a cash transaction, that the purchasers went immediately into possession; and it was held that these facts made the purchasers liable for interest because nothing was alleged to justify postponement of the maturity of the purchase-money.
It is also pointed out in that case that, although the contract allowed the purchasers a reasonable time to obtain a survey, yet the purchasers were not entitled to escape liability for interest upon that ground, "since they do not show that any time was desired or needed for that purpose." The opinion further states that the petitioners did not claim any delay whatever on account of the making of a survey. In the instant case, it is clearly alleged that by mutual consent the time for payment of the purchase-price was postponed until the timber lease expired.
While Code 37-104 would require the petitioner to do equity by tendering the amount admittedly due in order to obtain the relief sought, the petitioner offered to do equity by paying the full amount of the purchase-price, but the defendant, as alleged in the petition, repudiated the contract and declared his intentions not to carry out the contract. This was a waiver of tender, and under the law relieved the petitioner of any obligation to tender. Black v. Milner Hotels, 194 Ga. 828, 832 (22 S. E. 2d, 780).
From what has been said above, it must be held that the petition was not subject to the demurrer upon the ground that the petitioner failed to tender interest on the purchase-money. the special demurrer has not been argued and is therefore abandoned. No ruling is made on the exception to the judgment overruling it.
Judgment affirmed. All the Justices concur.
Nelson & Nelson, contra.
E. L. Stephens, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED JANUARY 13, 1950.
