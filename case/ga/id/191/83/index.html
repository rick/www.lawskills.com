REID v. WILSON.
17517.
(208 Ga. 235)
(65 SE2d 913)
(1951)
Probate of will. Before Judge Hendrix. Fulton Superior Court. April 4, 1951.
ALMAND, Justice.
1. Opinions of subscribing witnesses to a will as to the sanity of the testator at the time of the execution of the will are admissible without the witnesses' stating facts upon which they are founded. Potts v. House, 6 Ga. 324 (3) (50 Am. D. 329); Whitfield v. Pitts, 205 Ga. 259, 271 (53 S. E. 2d, 549). This ruling is not contrary to the ruling in Smoot v. Alexander, 188 Ga. 203 (3 S. E. 2d, 593), for the reason that, as ap pears from the official record, the witness in that case testifying as to the soundness of mind of the testator was not an attesting witness to the purported will. It was not error to admit the testimony complained of in special ground 1.
2. A court of ordinary in probating wills merely adjudicates the factum of the will, and a ground of caveat that the bequest in the purported will is too indefinite to be capable of ascertainment, contradictory in its terms, and void and unenforceable, constitutes no valid objection to the probate of the will. Trustees of the University of Georgia v. Denmark, 141 Ga. 390 (2) (81 S. E. 238); Johnson v. Ellis, 172 Ga. 435 (2) (158 S. E. 39); Peavey v. Crawford, 182 Ga. 782 (3) (183 S. E. 13, 107 A. L. R. 828). There was no error in admitting the purported will in evidence, as complained of in special ground 2.
3. It was not error to reject the proffered testimony as to the declarations of the testator shortly before his death, of which complaint is made in special grounds 3, 4, and 5.
4. The directed verdict in favor of the will was demanded by the evidence, and it was not error to overrule the caveator's motion for a new trial.
Frank Wilson, the nominated executor, offered for probate in the Court of Ordinary of Fulton County the purported last will and testament of Sanford S. Walker. J. D. Reid, one of the heirs at law of the testator, filed his caveat to the probate, alleging: (a) the instrument offered for probate is not the last will of Sanford S. Walker; (b) the execution of the will was induced by undue influence of the officers and agents of Grady Hospital and the City of Atlanta; (c) the instrument was void for uncertainty, because it attempted to make a bequest "for the poor and needy of Fulton County, Ga., and the City of Atlanta, who suffer from disease and for medical attention," and is too indefinite to be capable of ascertainment; (d) the instrument is contradictory in its terms and the contradictions are irreconcilable; (e) the pretended will is unreasonable and unjust as related to the rights of the next of kin including the caveator, in that said Sanford S. Walker died leaving a half sister, nephews and nieces, and attempting to leave his entire estate to strangers was sufficient to invoke the presence of undue influence and manifest the existence of mental weakness sufficient to support the presumption of the existence of mental incapacity; (f) a few days before his death said Sanford S. Walker expressed dissatisfaction with said pretended will, and stated that he was going to make another will. On the hearing before the ordinary, the court entered an order probating the will in solemn form. The caveator filed an appeal to the Superior Court, and on the trial of the appeal the court directed a verdict in favor of the will and against the caveat. The caveator's motion for a new trial, upon the general and special grounds, being overruled, the case is here assigning error on the judgment denying the caveator's motion for a new trial.
Special ground 1 complains that the court erred in refusing to sustain the caveator's objection to the following question propounded to Mrs. Lucile Smedley, one of the witnesses to the execution of the will offered by the propounders, and her answer thereto--viz: Q. "Would you mind stating whether or not in your opinion Mr. Sanford S. Walker was of sound mind at the time of the execution of that document?" A. "He impressed me as being of sound mind"--the objection being that the question sought a conclusion of the witness without the witness giving sufficient facts to support the conclusion. The two other attesting witnesses testified without objection that Walker was of sound mind at the time he executed the will.
Special ground 2 complains that the court erred in admitting in evidence the purported will of Sanford S. Walker, on the grounds: (a) it was indefinite, uncertain, and insufficient to transfer any property; (b) it was contradictory; and (c) the bequest and devise, which excluded near relatives, was sufficient to invoke the presence of undue influence.
Special grounds 3, 4, 5, and 6 will be stated in the opinion.
1, 2. Headnotes 1 and 2 do not require any elaboration.
3. Grounds 3, 4, and 5 will be considered together. They all relate to the refusal of the court to permit a witness for the caveator to testify as to a certain conversation that the witness had with the testator shortly before his death and more than two years after the execution of the purported will, in regard to his dissatisfaction with the will; that Grady Hospital would not take care of him, and that he desired to change the will. The objection to the testimony was that the same was immaterial and irrelevant, because it appears that the caveator did not claim that the will had been revoked, and any evidence of a desire to change the will or the part of the testator without proof of proper revocation was immaterial. The caveator insisted that the testimony was admissible, (a) to show undue influence, (b) to contradict the testimony of the propounder that the testator had stated to another that the will was freely made, and (c) to show that the testator did not know the contents of the alleged will at the time of its execution, and to show the presence of undue influence.
Pursley, 170 Ga. 788, 795 (154 S. E. 325). The rejected testimony concerned a remark made by the testator a few months before his death and more than two years after the execution of the will, and throws no light on the condition of the testator's mind at the time of the execution of the will. As to whether his mental condition was such that he could be easily influenced, the caveator introduced no evidence that any one sought to influence the testator in executing the will, and the only evidence as to mental condition at the time of the execution of the will was the testimony of the three subscribing witnesses, he was of sound mind, and the facts and circumstances show that the will was voluntarily executed by the testator. The only evidence for the caveator as to the testator's mental condition was that for a short period before his death he was old and feeble.
4. Ground 6 complains that the court erred in directing a verdict in favor of the will, because (a) the will was unreasonable, (b) there was evidence of undue influence, and (c) the evidence was sufficient to raise an issue of mental incapacity of the testator, which should have been submitted to the jury.
The propounder's evidence made a prima facie case that the testator was of sound mind at the time of the execution of the will, and that the will was duly executed and entitled to probate. There is a total lack of evidence on behalf of the caveator as to undue influence or mental incapacity of the testator at the time of the execution of the will, and consequently there was no issue to be submitted to the jury. It was not error for the court to direct a verdict in favor of the will. See Griffin v. Barrett, 183 Ga. 152 (187 S. E. 828); Watkins v. Jones, 184 Ga. 831 (193 S. E. 889); Martin v. Martin, 185 Ga. 349 (195 S. E. 159). The evidence demanded the verdict returned, and there was no error in overruling the motion for a new trial.
Judgment affirmed. All the Justices concur.
Spalding, Sibley, Troutman & Kelley, and James M. Sibley, contra.
Robert B. Blackburn and S. T. Allen, for plaintiff in error.
ARGUED JUNE 13, 1951 -- DECIDED JULY 9, 1951 -- REHEARING DENIED JULY 24, 1951.
