WILLIAMS et al. v. O'CONNOR et al.
17404.
(208 Ga. 39)
(64 SE2d 890)
(1951)
Ejectment. Before Judge Perryman. Lincoln Superior Court. December 14, 1950.
DUCKWORTH, Chief Justice.
1. Under the act of 1941 (Ga. L. 1941, pp. 487-489), the deed to secure debt upon which the plaintiffs rely in this common-law suit in ejectment should have been excluded from the evidence when objected to, because the debt secured was more than 20 years past due and the above act provides that in such a case title in the deed reverts to the grantor.
2. Since title was in the grantee from the date of the deed in 1929 until his death in 1938, all demises being laid on January 1, 1930, at a time when none of the lessors could have had title, a verdict in favor of the defendant was demanded. Furthermore, there being no proof of assent to legacies or that there was no administration or that the administrator had consented to the bringing of this suit by the heirs, there was a fatal deficiency in the evidence whether title is claimed as legatees or as heirs.
On March 29, 1929, W. A. Reid of Lincoln County, Georgia, conveyed a tract of land containing 159.3 acres more or less, located in the 182nd District, Lincoln County, Georgia, to Thomas O'Connor of Waterford, New York, to secure a debt of $500 due January 1, 1930, as stated in the deed. This deed was under seal, contained a general warranty of title with power of sale, and passed absolute title to the grantee, his heirs and assigns. On July 22, 1931, a series of five concurrent security deeds was executed by W. A. Reid and his two brothers to secure a lumber of promissory notes made previously. Each of these deeds embraced the 159.3 acres deeded previously to O'Connor, but recited that the conveyances were made subject to this prior deed. On March 7, 1950, pursuant to the power of sale contained in the five security deeds, the 159.3-acre tract, less 15.53 acres condemned by the United States Government, was sold to R. B. and J. T. Williams, the plaintiffs in error. On April 12, 1950, a suit in ejectment in the common-law form was filed by the defendants in error who are the heirs of Thomas O'Connor, the grantee of the prior security deed, against the purchasers at the foreclosure sale. At the trial of the case, after all the security deeds were submitted in evidence as well as proof that the plaintiffs were the heirs of O'Connor, the court directed the verdict in favor of the defendants in error. A motion for new trial, as amended, was overruled and the exception is to that judgment.
The only ground of the motion insisted on, aside from the general grounds, asserts that the court erred in allowing in evidence the security deed of W. A. Reid to Thomas O'Connor, because the title had reverted to the original grantor, his heirs and assigns, under the provisions of Code (Ann. Supp.) 67-1308 through 67-1315 (Ga. L. 1941, pp. 487-489), and that because of this error the court erred in directing the verdict.
This act of the legislature is presumed to be valid until and unless it is held invalid by a court when attacked in that court in the manner prescribed by law. It clearly applies to the deed here involved, and, since by its express provisions title in that deed had reverted to the grantor, the deed had ceased to be a valid conveyance of the land. Therefore, the deed should have been excluded, and it was error to allow its introduction in evidence over the objection.
The brief of counsel in this court strongly assails the 1941 act upon constitutional grounds and contends that, since the act is for those reasons unconstitutional and void, the deed was perfectly valid, and it was not error to allow its introduction in evidence. This court has repeatedly held that the constitutionality of a law can not be passed upon by the Supreme Court unless it has been first raised in the trial court by showing wherein the law offends specified portions of the Constitution and a ruling of the trial court has been made thereon. Morris v. State, 200 Ga. 471 (37 S. E. 2d, 345), and cases cited. There was no such attack made upon the 1941 act, and, accordingly, it follows that the act is presumed valid and the deed was invalid as evidence of title. Therefore, the court erred as contended in allowing it to be introduced in evidence.
2. Since the deed dealt within the preceding division is the source of the plaintiffs' title, and since we there held that it was, under the 1941 act, no evidence of title--thus holding that the plaintiffs failed to prove title to the land claimed--that ruling alone would require a reversal on the general grounds, since the verdict for the plaintiffs was not authorized by the evidence. But the reversal means that there will likely be another trial, and therefore it seems that we should rule upon other questions presented by the general grounds, for the guidance of the trial court in that trial.
The petitioners elected to bring their action in the common-law or fictitious form of ejectment. The case must therefore be tried in conformity with the rules of law applicable to that procedure. All demises are laid on January 1, 1930. To enable the plaintiffs to recover, since the plea of not guilty controverted their title to the premises, it was incumbent upon them to prove that they had legal title to the property in dispute at the time laid in the declaration, to wit, January 1, 1930. Scisson v. McLaws, 12 Ga. 166; Foster v. Stapler, 64 Ga. 766; Roberts v. Tift, 136 Ga. 901 (72 S. E. 234). This was not shown, but, on the contrary, it was shown that title on that date was in Thomas O'Connor and remained in him until his death in 1938. The evidence on this point demanded a verdict for the defendants.
A further ground for reversal is that there was no proof of assent by the executors to any legacy, nor was there any proof that there was no administration or that the administrator had consented to the bringing of this suit by the heirs. While there is no evidence to show whether the plaintiffs are claiming as legatees or as heirs, they could not prevail as legatees because assent to the legacies is not shown. Code, 113-801; Peoples National Bank v. Cleveland, 117 Ga. 908 (44 S. E. 20); Willingham v. Watson, 165 Ga. 870 (142 S. E. 458); Walker v. Horton, 184 Ga. 429 (191 S. E. 462); American Telephone & Telegraph Co. v. Smith, 196 Ga. 626 (27 S. E. 2d, 305). They could not prevail as heirs because they neither show that there is no administration nor that the administrator has consented to their bringing this suit. Greenfield v. McIntyre, 112 Ga. 691 (38 S. E. 44); Clark v. Woody, 197 Ga. 683 (30 S. E. 2d, 181); Bowman v. Bowman, 206 Ga. 262 (56 S. E. 2d, 497). These deficiencies in the evidence demanded a verdict for the defendants. It follows that the general grounds should have been sustained, and the court erred in overruling the motion for new trial as amended.
Judgment reversed. All the Justices concur.
Clement E. Sutton, contra.
Nathaniel E. Parker Jr., and Grant, Wiggins, Grizzard & Smith, for persons at interest.
Earle Norman and L. C. Groves, for plaintiffs in error.
