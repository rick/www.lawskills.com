PATTERSON v. CORRELL.
35651.
(92 Ga. App. 214)
(88 SE2d 327)
(1955)
Action on contract. Before Judge Hendrix. Fulton Superior Court. November 1, 1954.
GARDNER, P. J.
The court did not err in overruling the general demurrers of the defendant.
W. B. Correll III (whom we shall call the plaintiff) filed a suit in the Superior Court of Fulton County against W. L. Patterson (whom we shall call the defendant), seeking damages for misrepresentation and fraud in the sale to the plaintiff of two building lots. The defendant filed his general demurrers to the petition. The judge overruled the demurrers. To this judgment the defendant files exceptions here.
The petition alleged that the plaintiff made contact with the defendant, informing the defendant that he would buy two building lots provided the plaintiff could secure a permit from the authorities of the City of Atlanta permitting the building of two houses. The plaintiff specifically asked the defendant if a permit for building the houses could be secured without difficulty. The defendant assured the plaintiff that a permit could be secured for building the two houses without difficulty. The petition alleged that the plaintiff relied on the statement of the defendant and arranged for the financing of the erection of the two houses on the lots. After the plaintiff had arranged for the financing of the houses, he again contacted the defendant on April 28, 1954, and purchased the two lots from the defendant. The plaintiff, so the petition alleges, went to the "proper authorities" of the City of Atlanta to secure permits for building the houses on the lots acquired by the plaintiff from the defendant. The plaintiff was informed by W. B. Nixon, City Sewerage Engineer, that he (Nixon) would not approve the erection of the houses on the two lots in question due to the fact that the size and condition of the underground sewerage system beneath the two lots would not permit houses to be built over such system. The petition further alleged that Nixon advised the plaintiff that the defendant had been informed by Nixon that they (the city authorities) would not approve a permit for houses to be built on the two lots because of the size and condition of the underground sewerage system beneath the lots. The petition also alleged that Nixon informed the plaintiff that the defendant had been so advised at a date prior to April 28, 1954, the date on which the defendant sold the plaintiff the two lots. The petition alleges that the defendant did acts to deprive the plaintiff of his money and to give the plaintiff nothing of value in return for the same.
Code 105-302 reads: "Wilful misrepresentation of a material fact, made to induce another to act, and upon which he does act to his injury, will give a right of action. Mere concealment of such a fact, unless done in such a manner as to deceive and mislead, will not support an action. In all cases of deceit, knowledge of the falsehood constitutes an essential element. A fraudulent or reckless representation of facts as true, which the party may not know to be false, if intended to deceive, is equivalent to a knowledge of the falsehood." Code 96-203 reads: "Concealment of material facts may in itself amount to a fraud-- . . . 2. When, from any reason, one party has a right to expect full communication of the facts from the other." Code 37-703 reads: "Misrepresentation of a material fact, made wilfully to deceive, or recklessly without knowledge, and acted on by the opposite party, or if made by mistake and innocently and acted on by the opposite party, constitutes legal fraud."
An interesting decision in connection with the issue now being considered will be found in Foster v. Sikes, 202 Ga. 122 (42 S. E. 2d 441). While that case is not on all fours as compared to the facts in the case now before us, it is analogous to the case at bar in many respects. That case was carried from the Court of Appeals to the Supreme Court by certiorari, and the Supreme Court reversed the decision of the Court of Appeals and discussed the case fully. In Beavers v. Williams, 199 Ga. 113, 128 (33 S. E. 2d 343), the Supreme Court said: "Section 37-702 provides: 'Fraud may be actual or constructive. Actual fraud consists in any kind of artifice by which another is deceived.' Section 96-202, which was doubtless patterned after the statement in Smith v. Mitchell [6 Ga. 458], provides: 'Fraud may exist from misrepresentations by either party, made with design to deceive, or which does actually deceive the other party.' It is clear that the gravamen of the offense is the deceit which is practiced." We call attention to Cosby v. Asher, 74 Ga. App. 884 (41 S. E. 2d 793). That case is relied on by the defendant to sustain his position. We do not think the contention is sound under the facts of the instant case. We quote from Cosby v. Asher as follows: "Let us therefore gage the petition by the essential and necessary elements which must be alleged and proved in an action for fraud and deceit. The decisions of our court are clear and specific as to what these elements are: (a) that the representations were made by the defendant; (b) that they were knowingly and with design false; (c) that they were made for the purpose and intent to deceive and defraud; (d) that they did deceive and defraud; (e) that they related to an existing or past fact; (f) that the party to whom the false statements were made did not know that they where false; (g) that he relied on their truth and suffered a loss." Upon a careful reading of the facts in that case and the instant case, while the law involved is the same, the facts are in no way similar. That case is the only authority called to our attention by the defendant. In the instant case, upon inquiry by the plaintiff to the defendant, the plaintiff was told specifically that he could obtain a permit, and the defendant, for the purpose of making a sale to the plaintiff, stated that the plaintiff could get the permit, when, at the time the defendant made the statement and before the deed was executed and before the purchase price for the lots passed from the plaintiff to the defendant, the defendant knew that the plaintiff could not obtain such permit, and that the authorities of the City of Atlanta had so informed the defendant. This makes a set of facts quite different from those set out in Cosby v. Asher, supra.
The court did not err in overruling the general demurrers of the defendant.
Judgment affirmed. Townsend and Carlisle, JJ., concur.
John B. Griffin, contra.
Johnson, Hatcher & Meyerson, Henry M. Hatcher, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED MAY 31, 1955 -- REHEARING DENIED JUNE 15, 1955.
