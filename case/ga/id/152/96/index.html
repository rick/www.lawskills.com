PIEDMONT COTTON MILLS, INC. et al. v. GENERAL WAREHOUSE NO. TWO, INC.
23435.
(222 Ga. 164)
(149 SE2d 72)
(1966)
Injunction. Fulton Superior Court. Before Judge Tanksley.
COOK, Justice.
1. It was not error to overrule the general demurrers to the petition, which alleged the diversion of a stream from its channel by the defendants on the property of the defendant corporation, resulting in injury to the property of the petitioner by flooding.
2. The allegation that an officer and agent of the defendant corporation was acting within the scope of his authority in the commission of a tort charged the corporate defendant with the tort.
3. The proper measure of damages, under the allegations of the petition and the evidence in the case, was the diminution in market value, plus any reasonable and necessary expense incurred in minimizing the damage to the property of the petitioner.
4. The evidence authorized the verdict for the petitioner, and the award of actual and punitive damages against the defendants.
5. The trial judge did not err in refusing to accept the first verdict of the jury, which found actual damages against one defendant and punitive damages against the other defendant, nor in requiring them to return for further deliberations, with instructions that they could not find punitive damages against a defendant without finding some amount of actual damages.
6. The finding of the jury for the petitioner, with an award of actual and punitive damages against the defendants, authorized the trial judge to enter a judgment for permanent injunction in favor of the petitioner against the defendants.
7. The assertions in specifications of error Nos. 7, 8, 9, and 11, that the judge erred in failing to charge certain issues, without request to charge thereon, are without merit.
8. The charge that the petitioner would not be liable in damages for acts of the petitioner in concentrating surface water on its property, which would have caused the corporate defendant no injury if it had not wrongfully diverted the stream into which the surface waters flowed, was not erroneous.
9. The charge complained of in specification of error No. 16, in regard to damages, was not error.
General Warehouse No. Two, Inc., brought an action for injunction and damages against T. W. Tift and Piedmont Cotton Mills, Inc. It was alleged: The petitioner purchased from the defendant corporation a described tract of land for a cash consideration of $149,350, which tract was carved from a larger tract owned by the defendant corroboration. Since the purchase of the property, the petitioner has constructed thereon a seven-story office building, which is leased to the Federal Aviation Agency. At the time the petitioner purchased the described property on February 13, 1962, a stream ran in a channel through property of the defendant corporation, which adjoins the petitioner's property, and flowed in a southeasterly direction, crossing a strip of land, 30 feet in width and 20 feet in length, which connects the main body of the property of the petitioner with Willingham Drive. In the early part of October, 1962, a contractor engaged by the petitioner to build the office building, and to make other improvements incidental thereto, proceeded to build a concrete bridge across the stream at the south end of the 30-foot strip of land, preparatory to the paving of a roadway within the strip between the main tract of the petitioner's property and Willingham Drive. On October 5, 1962, described construction had been completed on this bridge, and on the afternoon of that date the defendant Tift demanded that the petitioner's contractor move the bridge southward, stating that he intended to change the stream so that half of it would be on the property of the petitioner; he further informed the petitioner's contractor that if work did not cease immediately, he would tear out the construction that had been completed. On that evening the defendant Tift, "individually and as an officer and agent of Piedmont Cotton Mills, Inc., acting within the scope of his authority, did wilfully, wantonly and maliciously go onto the property of your petitioner and with the use of a tractor and cables did tear down, demolish and destroy the work that had been done on the aforesaid bridge." Several plays after the defendants demolished the petitioner's construction work, they graded out a ditch along the northern boundary of the petitioner's property, and then proceeded to fill the former watercourse with soil, thereby diverting the flow of the stream from its former channel into the ditch made by them. A large quantity of surface water flows into this stream during rains, but prior to the diversion of the stream, it had been able to accommodate this water. Since the diversion of the stream, the artificial bed created by the defendants does not contain the water after each rain, and the water flows over, onto, and against the property of the petitioner, causing it to erode and wash away. The changing of the direction of the stream causes it to flow into the culverts beneath the bridge of the petitioner at a different angle, which impedes the flow of the stream to the extent that during heavy rains a lake is created on the west side of the bridge, which overflows the roadway at this point, and the roadbed is being washed away from beneath the paving, leaving the roadway in a dangerous condition. When the waters recede, mud and debris are left on the petitioner's property. The flooding caused by the diversion of the stream has eroded the petitioner's property along the south side of the stream as it now exists for a distance of approximately 150 feet to a width of three feet in places, thereby undermining a fence created along the northern side of the petitioner's property; and the earth is beginning to wash away from beneath the petitioner paved parking area in changing the natural flow of the stream the defendants have created a continuing nuisance and trespass on the petitioner's property, and unless restrained and enjoined, the defendants will continue to allow it to so flow, to the petitioner's continuing damage. The value of the real property of the petitioner, without regard to the improvements thereon, has been diminished as follows: Prior to the acts of the defendants, the property was of the reasonable value of $149,350; because of the acts of the defendants, the reasonable value of the property is now $124,350. In order to prevent future damage by reason of the diversion of the stream, the petitioner has been
required to spend $6,773 to insert metal tiling, $250 to repave the surface undermined by the stream, and $240 to insert a concrete curb.
The petitioner prayed that the defendants be temporarily restrained and permanently enjoined from allowing the waters to continue flowing on, against, and upon the petitioner's property; that the petitioner have judgment against the defendants in the sum of $32,263 for actual damages, and $25,000 as punitive damages; that it be awarded attorney's fees in the sum of $10,000 and for other relief.
Answers and general special demurrers were filed by the defendants. The defendant corporation filed a cross-action for damages in the amount of $125,000 for alleged flooding of its land by the petitioner. The first trial of the case resulted in a mistrial. On the second trial the jury returned a verdict in favor of the petitioner, and awarded $13,500 actual damages, and $5,500 punitive damages against both defendants. On June 1, 1965, judgment was entered for the damages, and it was further adjudged that the defendants be permanently enjoined. The defendants filed a motion for new trial on the general grounds, which was amended by the addition of two special grounds.
The defendants filed a joint appeal from the judgment entered on the verdict of the jury, and from the denial of their motion for new trial. In the first four specifications of the enumeration of errors, it is asserted that it was error to overrule general and special demurrers of the defendants. The fifth specification assigns as error the denial of the motion for new trial, as amended. Eleven additional errors are asserted in the enumeration of errors.
1. It was not error to overrule the general demurrers to the petition, which alleged the diversion of a stream from its channel by the defendants on the property of the defendant corporation, resulting in injury to the property of the petitioner by flooding. Code 85-1301, 105-1407.
2. The only special demurrers urged by the defendants are those attacking the allegations in regard to a malicious tort of the defendants, and the allegations with reference to the measure of damages claimed by the petitioner. It is alleged that the defendant T. W. Tift "individually and as an officer and agent of" the corporate defendant, "acting within the scope of his authority," demolished the portion of construction completed on a bridge on the petitioner's property, after previously demanding that the bridge be moved. It is asserted that the allegations do not charge this tort to the corporate defendant because it does not affirmatively appear that the initial demand was authorized by the corporation or within the scope of Tift's authority, or that the acts were ratified by the corporation.
"A principal may be liable for the wilful tort of his agent, done in the prosecution and within the scope of his business, although it is not expressly shown that he either commanded the commission of the wilful act or assented to it." Planters Cotton-Oil Co. v. Baker, 181 Ga. 161, 163 (181 SE 671); Frazier v. Southern R. Co., 200 Ga. 590 (37 SE2d 774). The allegation that Tift, as an officer and agent of the defendant corporation, was acting within the scope of his authority, was sufficient to charge the corporation with the tort of Tift, and the petition was not subject to the special demurrers interposed in regard to this tort.
In the present case the petition alleged permanent damage to the property of the petitioner because of the erosion that had already resulted from the diversion of the stream by the defendants, and damages were prayed for diminution in market value and for expenses sustained in preventing further damage. The recovery of these damages was authorized under the allegations of the petition. That the petitioner prayed for injunction to prevent further injury to its property did not show that the nuisance was a temporary and abatable one, which could be compensated only to the extent of the depreciation in rental value of the property. For cases dealing with the measure of damages in cases of permanent and nonpermanent injuries by nuisance see: Hodges v. Pine Product Co., 135 Ga. 134 (68 SE 1107, 33 LRA (NS) 74, 21 AC 1052); Atkinson v. Kreis, 140 Ga. 52 (2) (78 SE 465); Central Georgia Power Co. v. Pope, 141 Ga. 186 (2) (80 SE 642, LRA 1916D 358); Sweetman v. Owens, 147 Ga. 436 (94 SE 542); Barham v. Grant, 185 Ga. 601 (196 SE 43). It was not error to overrule the special demurrers objecting to the measure of damages alleged by the petitioner.
In specification 12 of the enumeration of errors it is asserted that the judge erred in charging the jury as to the measure of damages when the petitioner might recover against the defendants. The judge correctly charged that if the petitioner should be entitled to recover damages, it would be entitled to recover the difference between the fair market value of the land immediately before the stream was diverted from its normal channel, and the fair market value at the time the petition was filed, plus any reasonable and necessary expense incurred in minimizing the damage to its land.
4. The evidence on the trial was in sharp conflict. There was an abundance of evidence to support the allegations of the petition that the defendants had diverted the stream from the channel in which the defendant corporation, and that this diversion of the stream had caused the flooding of the land of the petitioner. There was evidence for the defendants that the defendant corporation had merely cleared out the channel of the stream, and had not changed its course. The jury was authorized to find for the petitioner, and it was not error to overrule the general grounds of the motion for new trial.
Special ground 4 of the motion for new trial is an elaboration of the general grounds. The appellants contend that the evidence did not support the award of $13,500 actual damages and $5,500 punitive damages. The evidence showed that the petitioner had spent nearly $8,000 in repairing and preventing damage caused by the erosion resulting from the diversion of the stream. There was sufficient evidence from which the jury was authorized to find declaration in the marked value of the land in an amount equal to the difference between the actual expenses proved and the $13,500 actual damages awarded by the jury. The evidence concerning the wilful nature of the trespass authorized a finding for punitive damages. It was not error to overrule this ground of the motion for new trial.
5. Ground 5 of the motion for new trial shows that the first verdict of the jury was a finding in favor of the petitioner, with an award of actual damages of $13,500 against Piedmont Cotton Mills, Inc., and an award of $5,500 punitive damages against T. W. Tift. The trial judge declined to accept the verdict in this form. After further instructions, the jury returned a verdict in favor of the petitioner, awarding $13,500 actual damages and $5,500 punitive damages against both defendants. It is asserted that the first verdict was in proper form, and published as provided by law, and that it could not thereafter be reconsidered by the jury and additional damages awarded.
Code 105-2002 provides: "In every tort there may be aggravating circumstances, either in the act or the intention, and in that event the jury may give additional damages, either to deter the wrongdoer from repeating the trespass or as compensation for the wounded feelings of the plaintiff." In Foster v. Sikes, 202 Ga. 122, 126 (42 SE2d 441), this court held: "While punitive damages may be given in a proper case even though the actual damage is small, in order for the plaintiff to be entitled to such damages there must be some injury which would support an action for at least nominal damages. A tort must have been committed before punitive damages can be assessed. Since no injury is shown to the plaintiff in the present case, he has no cause of action for punitive damages." The Foster case was dealing with pleadings, but the principle announced would be applicable to the verdict of the jury. Code 105-2002 refers to punitive damages as "additional" damages, indicating that it is only where the jury returns a verdict for actual damages that punitive damages may be found.
Since the first verdict of the jury was improper and inconsistent, finding actual damages against one defendant and punitive damages against the other defendant, the trial judge properly required the jury to return for further deliberations, with instructions that they could not find punitive damages without finding some amount as actual damages. South v. Pilcher, 130 Ga. 350 (60 SE 1000); Lowery v. Morton, 200 Ga. 227 (36 SE2d 664); Fried v. Fried, 208 Ga. 861 (3a) (69 SE2d 862); Parrish Bakeries of Georgia, Inc. v. Wiseman Baking Co., 104 Ga. App. 573, 575 (122 SE2d 260).
In Anderson. v. Sapp, 135 Ga. 204 (69 SE 181), the petition sought to enjoin an impending trespass and to recover damages for acts already committed. The following verdict was returned: "We, the jury, find for plaintiff, and sixty-five dollars for timber cut." This court held that this verdict "was a finding by the jury that the plaintiff was entitled to the writ of injunction as prayed, and damages in the stated amount." Compare Gray v. Junction City Manufacturing Co., 195 Ga. 33 (3) (22 SE2d 847).
The trial judge in the present case did not err in entering a judgment for permanent injunction on the verdict of the jury.
7. Specifications of error Nos. 7, 8, 9, and 11 contend that the trial judge erred in failing to charge certain issues, without requests to charge thereon. We have examined these specifications and they are without merit.
8. In specification of error No. 15 it is asserted that the judge erred in charging the jury that, "if Piedmont Cotton Mills, Inc. diverted the stream from its former channel and had this not occurred the stream would have naturally carried away any surface water which was collected and concentrated by General Warehouse No. Two, Inc., that the same would have carried the water away, that there would be no liability on the plaintiff because the diversion of the stream would, in effect, have caused the damage."
The trial judge charged extensively on the proximate cause of damages as applied to the injuries claimed by both the petitioner and the defendant corporation. It was not error to charge, in effect, that the collection and concentration of surface water by the petitioner and the discharge thereof in the natural stream, would not make the petitioner liable for damages to the defendant corporation if the discharge was not beyond the capacity of the watercourse flowing in its natural channel. See 93 CJS 816, Waters, 117.
9. The charge complained of in specification of error No. 16, in regard to damages, was not erroneous.
