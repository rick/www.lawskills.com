DAVIS et al. v. HARNESBERGER et al.
18936.
(211 Ga. 625)
(87 SE2d 841)
(1955)
Equitable petition. Before Judge Perryman. Lincoln Superior Court. January 22, 1955.
WYATT, Presiding Justice.
Mrs. Louise Harnesberger Davis and B. I. Harnesberger filed their petition against Guy William Harnesberger, J. O. Harnesberger, Mrs. Frances Harnesberger McCoy, Mrs. Nellie Mae Harnesberger DeMers, and Thomas A. Harnesberger in which they prayed for an equitable partitioning of described lands and an accounting for rents and profits. It was alleged in substance that the described lands were set apart as a year's support to Mrs. B. T. Harnesberger and her six minor children in 1923 and that the said minor children were Mrs. Louise Harnesberger Davis and B. I. Harnesberger who were the plaintiffs in the courts below and four of the defendants, to wit: Guy William Harnesberger, Mrs. Frances Harnesberger McCoy, Mrs. Nellie Mae Harnesberger DeMers, and Thomas A. Harnesberger; that Mrs. B. T. Harnesberger conveyed a portion of the land in question to J. O. Harnesberger on January 8, 1944; that on September 14, 1946 and January 25, 1947, the remainder of the lands in question was conveyed by Mrs. B. T. Harnesberger to Guy Harnesberger; that all of the minor children were at the time of filing the suit sui juris and that Mrs. B. T. Harnesberger died on January 11, 1954; that there had been no administration of her estate and that all of her debts had been paid; that J. O. Harnesberger and Guy William Harnesberger were in possession of the lands in question and were claiming title to same based upon the deeds executed by Mrs. B. T. Harnesberger to them; that no application was made by Mrs. B. T. Harnesberger to the ordinary of the county in which the land in question was situated to sell the land prior to the time the deeds were executed by her to J. O. Harnesberger and Guy William Harnesberger.
Answers to the petition were filed by J. O. Harnesberger and Guy William Harnesberger in which they admitted as true the allegations of the petition above set out. They contended that title to the lands in question was vested in them by reason of seven years possession under color of title. They contended further that the lands were conveyed to them by their mother in pursuance to a valid agreement entered into by all of the children, their brothers and sisters, and their mother prior to her death and prior to the execution of the deeds to them. Demurrer to the answers were overruled, and upon the trial, a verdict was rendered in favor of the defendants J. O. Harnesberger and Guy William Harnesberger. A motion for new trial was denied. The exception here is to the judgment overruling the demurrer to the answer and to the judgment denying a new trial.
1. Code (Ann. Supp.) 113-1025 provides as follows: "A conveyance or incumbrance of any or all the property set apart as a year's support for the joint benefit of the widow and her minor child or children shall convey or incumber and be binding and conclusive upon the child or children, and persons claiming through or under them, only when approved by the ordinary of the county in which all or a part of the property is located. No such approval shall be necessary to bind a child sui juris who joins with the widow in making the conveyance or incumbrance. The purchaser or lender shall not be responsible for the proper use of application of the proceeds." There is no contention in this case that there was any effort to comply with this Code section prior to the execution of the deeds in question.
See also Hansen v. Owens, 132 Ga. 648 (64 S. E. 800). "Where a tenant in common conveys the whole lot to a third person, and the grantee took possession, claiming the entire lot as his own, this action constitutes a disseizen and ouster of the other tenants in common and they are barred from asserting their right to such property after the expiration of seven years." Broadwater v. Parker, 209 Ga. 801 (76 S. E. 2d 402). This court has held that a void deed will operate as color of title. Smith v. Jefferson County, 201 Ga. 674 (40 S. E. 2d 773).
The jury in the instant case was authorized to find from the evidence that the defendants in the court below and defendants in error here had been in adverse possession of the property in question since the date the deeds were executed by their mother purporting to convey title to them. This was for a period of time of more than seven years. Clearly, under the rules of law above cited, the deeds in question could properly be relied upon as color of title.
We are not unmindful of the rule of law requiring an actual ouster of express notice of adverse possession in the case of cotenants. In the instant case, the jury was authorized to find that soon after the death of the father if all the parties to the case under consideration, the plaintiffs in error and the defendants in error met at the house of their mother who was then in life, and it was agreed by all the parties that, if any of the children would pay the debts of the father's estate and take care of the mother during her life, the child or children so doing might have title to the lands in question; and that the defendants in error complied with this agreement, and the deeds were accordingly executed by the mother to the defendants in error. Whether or not this agreement was sufficiently definite to be enforced as a verbal contract for the sale of land, it was sufficient to put the plaintiffs in error on notice that the defendants in error were in possession of the land claiming title to the entire property by virtue of the deeds executed to them, and was sufficient as to the cotenants.
2. It follows, the judgment overruling the demurrer to the answer setting up title by prescription was not error.
3. It becomes unnecessary to rule upon the questions raised as to the defense of a verbal contract for the sale of land.
Judgment affirmed. All the Justices concur.
Earle Norman, Ben B. Ross, L. C. Groves, contra.
Killebrew, McGahee & Plunkett, Colley & Orr, for plaintiffs in error.
