HOWARD v. THE STATE.
35927.
(93 Ga. App. 147)
(91 SE2d 118)
(1955)
Embezzlement. Before Judge Anderson. Bibb Superior Court. August 11, 1955.
GARDNER, P. J.
In a charge of embezzlement from a city, the evidence must show that at the time of the alleged embezzlement title to the property was vested in the city. On the other hand, if the property was obtained by the officer of the city by fraud the title never vested in the city, and if the defendant would be guilty of any offense it would be larceny rather than embezzlement.
The defendant was head of the electrical department of the City of Macon. As such officer he was originally charged with embezzlement on 17 counts. By reversal of the appellate court, by directions of the verdict in the trial court, by abandonment of counts, and by verdicts of not guilty there was left only one count against the defendant, which charged the defendant with embezzlement for that he did in his official capacity purchase one wall gas-furnace in the name of the city, which fixture he converted to his own use. He was convicted of embezzlement. He filed his motion for a new trial on the statutory grounds and thereafter added a special ground based on the failure of the court to give to the jury a timely written request to charge. The court overruled this motion and the defendant assigns error here on the amended motion.
The evidence reveals that the defendant as such officer of the city had the power to purchase electrical supplies for it. In the instant case he went to an electrical supply establishment and purchased in the name of the city the wall gas-furnace and carried it to his home. The record reveals that the city never used such equipment and had no use for such equipment in its affairs. It is the defendant's contention that while the equipment in question was charged to the city it was done by mistake; that it was his intention to purchase the equipment in question for his own personal use and not for the city; that during the course of the numerous transactions and invoices which passed through his hands by virtue of his office he did not detect that the item of $60.25 was charged to the city instead of to himself.
"I charge you with respect to count one, involving the wall furnace, if you believe that the defendant obtained possession of the furnace from Lowe Electric Co. by any trick or fraud, or under any false pretense that he was buying it for the City of Macon and he was to turn it over to the City of Macon, and at such time had the intent to appropriate the furnace to his own use, and that Lowe Electric Co. delivered the furnace over to the defendant for the delivery of the furnace by the defendant to the City of Macon, I charge you that the defendant would have obtained the furnace unlawfully; and if he thereafter appropriated the furnace in pursuance of such original intent, he is guilty of simple larceny, and not embezzlement, and you should acquit him as to this count."
It is elementary and needs no authority to sustain it, that in embezzlement cases the property must come into the hands of the officer lawfully. On the other hand, if it comes into the hands of the officer unlawfully it is simple larceny (or in some cases larceny after trust). The court should have charged the principles of law, under the record of this case, as set forth in the request. This was the sole defense of this defendant that he did not come into the possession of the property for the city but that he intended the purchase to be for himself and not for the city. It therefore follows as a matter of law that if the defendant obtained possession of the property by fraudulent means the title thereto never vested in the city but vested in the defendant. If the title to the property never vested in the city, the defendant could not have been guilty of embezzlement. Embezzlement applies only where the title to the property vested in the city. If thereafter the officer appropriates the property to his own use he will be guilty of embezzlement. If the court had given the principle of law as set out in the special request, the jury could have found, under this record, that if the defendant was guilty of any offense such offense would be only simple larceny. There are many decisions to this effect. We deem it unnecessary to cite them. The court erred in refusing to give the special request to charge, which was timely and applicable to the record in this case.
Since the case may be tried again we are not passing on the evidence regarding the general grounds.
Wm. M. West, Solicitor-General, Charles F. Adams, Assistant Solicitor-General, contra.
Miller, Miller & Miller, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED NOVEMBER 30, 1955 -- REHEARING DENIED DECEMBER 16, 1955.
