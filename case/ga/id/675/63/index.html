MURRAY v. STATE HIGHWAY DEPARTMENT et al.
38715.
(103 Ga. App. 517)
(120 SE2d 48)
(1961)
Condemnation. Marion Superior Court. Before Judge Thompson.
BELL, Judge.
2. (a) In a condemnation proceeding where the condemnor pays the amount of the assessors' award to the condemnee within the statutory time for appeal but after the appeal was undertaken, the payment is seasonably made.
(b) Under Code Ann. 95-1721 a county may make payment on behalf of the State Highway Department as condemnor.
3. An appeal from an assessors' award by the State or its political subdivisions as condemnor is not dismissable because of the failure of the condemnor to pay assessors' charges and costs prior to the appeal. Code 36-605.
The State Highway Department, as plaintiff and condemnor, filed an action for condemnation in the Superior Court of Marion County, Georgia, against a certain tract of land and E. N. Murray and Marion County Lumber Co., Inc., defendants and condemnees. Assessors were selected, who filed their award on October 6, 1959, that the State Highway Department should pay to E. N. Murray $29,834. On October 7 the trial judge entered and signed a judgment ordering and adjudging the property to be condemned in fee simple, providing that upon payment the State Highway Department was vested with fee-simple title to the property in question. The judgment of the court was filed in the office of the clerk of the superior court of the county on that same day.
On October 10, 1959, the State Highway Department filed in the office of the Clerk of the Superior Court of Marion County its appeal from the award to the superior court. On November 3, 1959, the condemnee filed his motion to dismiss the appeal. This motion was amended on June 4, 1960. On September 30, 1960, the State Highway Department, at a hearing upon the motion, made an oral motion to strike and dismiss the motion of the condemnee to dismiss the appeal. This motion of the condemnor was sustained, resulting in the dismissal of the motion of the condemnee to dismiss the appeal. To this order of the court the condemnee excepted on the grounds that it was error as a matter of law to strike and dismiss the motion to dismiss the appeal of the condemnor, and that it was error for the trial judge, in passing on the oral motion of the condemnor, to consider the deposition of the clerk of court and the stipulation of facts entered into by counsel representing the parties. In this respect the condemnee contends that the trial judge should have considered only the legal sufficiency of the allegations contained in the motion to dismiss the appeal; and, further, that the motion of the condemnee to dismiss the appeal was good in law and should not have been stricken and dismissed by the trial judge. Further assignments of error urge that the evidence considered by the trial judge, the deposition and stipulation of facts, did not authorize the trial judge to dismiss and strike the motion of the condemnee, but, rather, demanded that the trial judge overrule and "dismiss said oral motion to strike and dismiss said motion to dismiss said appeal"; that "condemnor did not, at or before the filing of said appeal in the office of the Clerk of Marion County Superior Court, pay to condemnee the amount of the award, from which condemnor appealed; nor pay any part of said amount to condemnee; nor tender said sum to condemnee, nor tender any part of said sum to condemnee. Condemnor did not, at or before the filing of said appeal in the office of said clerk, pay or tender the amount of said award, or any part thereof, to the Clerk of the Superior Court of Marion County, Georgia"; that "Said condemnor did not, at or before the filing of said appeal on October 10, 1959, pay the costs which had accrued in the case up to the time of filing said appeal"; that "At the time of the filing of said appeal on October 10, 1959, there were substantial costs which accrued in said cause, but they have not been paid by condemnor or by anyone else"; that "Condemnor has never paid the amount of the award, or any part thereof, into the registry of the court, which is contrary to 'the purpose and the mandatory requirement of' Georgia Laws 1914, pp. 92, et seq., as amended by Georgia Laws, 1937-1938, Ex. Session, pp. 251 et seq., all as codified in Chapter 36-11 of the Georgia Code of 1933, as amended"; that "The filing of its appeal by condemnor on October 10, 1959, without having, at or before such filing, paid or tendered to condemnee the amount of said award, or any part thereof, and without having, at or before such filing, paid the amount of said award, or any part
thereof, into the registry of the court, constituted a taking of the private property of the condemnee for public purposes, without just and adequate compensation being first paid to condemnee the same being in violation of, and contrary to, Article I, Section III, Paragraph I of the Constitution of the State of Georgia of 1945."
1. We consider initially the contention of the condemnee that in passing upon an oral motion to strike and dismiss the motion to dismiss the appeal, the trial court cannot consider the evidence. In support of this contention, condemnee urges that an oral motion to strike or dismiss the pleading is similar to or in the nature of a general demurrer and, therefore, no matters of evidence can be considered. While certainly this is the rule in cases testing the pleadings by a motion to strike, nevertheless a motion to dismiss an appeal, at least in condemnation proceedings, under Code Ch. 36-11, which is regulated by Code Ch. 36-6, falls in a different category from an oral motion to strike pleadings, amendments, or answers. Here the motion to dismiss the appeal raises issues of fact such as whether the condemnation money has been paid into the registry of the court as required by law. If the deposition of the clerk of the court relating to the issue of payment of condemnation money and of costs cannot be considered on the motion to dismiss the appeal, how is the judge to determine the issue? Common sense impels us to conclude that the deposition and stipulation were properly considered by the trial judge in this case. The deposition and stipulation here furnish the only means of setting forth proof of the facts upon which the respective parties based their contentions, the condemnor contending that the condemnee received payment, while the condemnee urges that the appeal had to be dismissed for lack of compliance with the required constitutional and statutory provisions for a valid appeal in condemnation cases.
2. The next issue relates to the problem in condemnation proceedings where an award of the assessors must have been paid as a condition precedent before an appeal can be taken by the condemnor. The statute, Code 36-1110, provides that the same right of appeal shall lie from an assessors' award to a jury in the superior court, as authorized in Ch. 36-6, and upon like terms and conditions in all respects as are therein sanctioned. Code 36-601 provides that in case either party or the representative of either party is dissatisfied, he or they may, within ten days from the time the award is filed, enter in writing an appeal from the award to the superior court where the award is filed. The record in this case reveals that the appeal by the State Highway Department was entered on October 10, 1959. The stipulation of facts shows that on October 9, 1959, the clerk of court handed to the condemnee a check in payment of the amount of the assessors award. The check was payable to the condemnees originally listed in the condemnation proceedings, namely, E. N. Murray and Marion County Lumber Co. E. N. Murray forwarded the check to Marion County Lumber Co., who returned it to Murray with the company endorsement upon it. The check was endorsed by Murray on October 13, 1959, and deposited that day to his account in the bank upon which it was drawn. While the stipulation of facts does not cover the matter, the perforations in the check indicate payment on October 14, 1959.
Numerous cases cited in the briefs of counsel are concerned with the question as to when a check is or is not payment and whether the payment relates back to the time of receipt of the check by the payee. However, here it is not necessary to pass upon these matters. "Under the decisions in Woodside v. City of AtLanta, 214 Ga. 75 (103 S. E. 2d 108), and State Highway Dept. v. Wilson, 98 Ga. App. 619 (106 S. E. 2d 544), as construed by the Supreme Court in State Highway Dept. v. Hendrix, 215 Ga. 821 (113 S. E. 2d 761), an appeal by a condemnor from the assessors' award to a jury in the superior court is valid only when the amount of the assessors' award is properly paid or tendered into the registry of the court within the statutory period in which appeals are allowed." State Highway Dept. v. Draper, 102 Ga. App. 199, 200 (115 S. E. 2d 590). The question for decision in the Draper case was whether or not an appeal was valid when it was filed before the amount of the assessors' award was paid into the registry of the court (both having been accomplished within the statutory period allowed for appeal). There the court held that while the appeal as originally filed under the cited decisions was invalid and subject to a motion to dismiss, the defect could have been waived by the condemnees by failing to properly object before a verdict by a jury, and that the jury's verdict, as with other amendable defects in pleadings, would forever cure such defect; that the defect in the appeal as originally filed, being one that would be cured by verdict, was amendable, and the payment of the assessors' award into the registry of the court within the statutory period allowed was tantamount to an amendment, and when the appeal was thus amended, it was not subject to the condemnees' motion to dismiss. In the Draper case the amount of the assessors' award was paid six days after the condemnor filed its appeal from the assessors' award to a jury. Both the appeal and the payment were within ten days of the judgment of the court on the award of the assessors. In the present case the money was not paid into the registry of the court (which, of course, would receive the payment for delivery to the condemnee), but was paid directly to the condemnee three days after the condemnor's appeal was filed and six days after the judgment of the court was entered on the assessors award. Under the authority of the Draper case the payment here was properly made directly to the condemnee, who indisputably received and accepted it within ten days after the judgment on the award. The fact that the check was drawn by the Marion County Commissioners we deem immaterial, in view of Code Ann. 95-1721, which authorizes the counties to make such payments. The fact that the award was made to E. N. Murray alone but the payment was made to him and the other condemnee is of no substantial consequence, since it is undisputed that the condemnee received the money after endorsement of the check by the other payee.
3. There remains one question to be determined, namely, whether the appeal should have been dismissed because of the failure of the condemnor to pay the costs. Code 36-605 requires that the person condemning shall pay the assessors' charges and such costs as shall be fixed in each case by the judge. However, as amended in 1955, the last sentence of this statute exempts the State of Georgia and its political subdivisions from the provisions of that section. Ga. L. 1955, pp. 651-2. Accordingly, the failure to pay costs within ten days from the judgment did not vitiate the appeal.
Judgment affirmed. Felton, C. J., and Nichols, J., concur.
Eugene Cook, Attorney-General, Carter Goode, E. J. Summerour, Assistant Attorneys-General, Joe Rogers, contra.
Hatcher, Smith, Stubbs & Rothschild, Albert W. Stubbs, for plaintiff in error.
