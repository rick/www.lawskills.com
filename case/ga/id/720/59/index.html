GEORGIA INSURANCE SERVICE et al. v. LORD.
33240.
(83 Ga. App. 28)
(62 SE2d 402)
(1950)
Appeal; from Glynn Superior Court-- Judge Thomas. May 16, 1950.
GARDNER, J.
Where a claimant for compensation was blind in his right eye, his employer hired him with actual knowledge that he was blind in his right eye, and such claimant sustained in injury to his left eye, which injury resulted from an accident arising out of and in the course of his employment, and resulted in the loss of the claimant's vision in the left eye, such claimant was not entitled to compensation for a total permanent disability because of his total blindness, but only to compensation for the loss of vision in his left eye.
In January, 1949, McCleskey C. Lord, while employed with the Georgia Insurance Service Inc., at Brunswick, Georgia, lost the sight of his left eye.
Lord had, prior to going to work with this company, lost the sight of his right eye. He was sixty-four years old at the time of the loss of the left eye. Therefore, when he entered the employment of this corporation, he could only see out of his left eye and this was known to his employer. Lord filed with the Workmen's Compensation Board his claim for compensation on account of the loss of the sight of his left eye. The board rendered an award in favor of the claimant, to wit, that his injury arose out of and in the course of his employment, and awarded compensations to him for the loss of the sight of his left eye, as provided by Code 114-406. There was evidence before the board, that the loss of the sight in this eye was due to a hemorrhage or rupture of a blood vessel therein, which took place while the claimant was actually engaged in performing his duties for his employer (doing book work late at night); that the claimant sold and collected for fire insurance over certain territory for said company during the daytime, that this hemorrhage resulted from the strain to which his eye was put in doing this book work; that the board also found that on said date the claimant was suffering from industrial blindness in his right eye and that this was in no way connected with or related to the accident causing him to lose the sight in his left eye; and that in fact, the previous injury to his right eye, causing him to lose the sight thereof, in no way aided or contributed to the subsequent injury to his left eye by which he lost the sight thereof. It was not disputed that the claimant was, therefore, totally blind and unable to perform any gainful occupation. The employer and the insurance carrier (United States Casualty Company) appealed from the award of the full board (approving the award of the director) to the Superior Court of Glynn County. It appears from the order of the judge of said superior court that the claimant likewise filed an appeal from the award to the superior court, although this appeal does not appear in the record. The superior court rendered judgment denying the appeal of the employer and the insurance carrier and granting and sustaining the appeal filed by the claimant, and set aside the order of the full board of December 12, 1949, and thereupon rendered an order and judgment awarding to the claimant compensation "on the basis of a total and permanent disability" and directed the payment of compensation to him at the rate of $20 a week for 350 weeks (instead of said sum weekly for 100 weeks, being for the total loss of the sight of his left eye) "for total and permanent disability" sustained by the claimant "which resulted in his total loss of vision as the result of an accident and injury arising out of and in the course of his employment," and for the employer to pay all medical expenses not exceeding $500. To this judgment the employer and the insurance carrier excepted.

The question whether the injury to the employee was caused by an accidental injury arising out of and in the course of the claimant's employment involves a question of fact, and there being no question of fraud here, such a finding by the board is conclusive if supported by the facts. See Home Ind. Co. v. Googe, 45 Ga. App. 302 (164 S. E. 479); Standard Accident Ins. Co. v. Kiker, 45 Ga. App. 706 (165 S. E. 850); Maryland Casualty Co. v. Brown, 48 Ga. App. 822 (173 S. E. 925).
The finding here cannot be said to be entirely without evidence to support it insofar as it found that the claimant employee sustained an injury which arose out of and in the course of his employment by reason of the fact that he had a hemorrhage in his left eye, caused by strain while at work for his employer, and immediately thereafter lost his sight in this eye. The claimant testified that his sight in this eye was not affected in any way prior to his doing this work late at night for his employer and that while so working he felt a sharp pain in this eye and that he has not been able to see out of the same since that time. Expert witnesses testified that claimant's sight, except that he could tell night from day, was gone from his left eye and that this loss of sight had been caused by a hemorrhage therein. It appeared that the previous permanent injury to his right eye, caused by glaucoma, in no way caused the condition in his left eye or affected the vision thereof. Dr. Hicks testified for the claimant to the effect that it was possible for a strain to have caused this hemorrhage, which strain resulted from working late at night over his books. He stated that their are cases on record where hemorrhages to the eye were caused by excessive strain, and that, assuming that the claimant could see before this hemorrhage in his left eye took place and could not see thereafter, he "would say it would have been the cause."
2. The superior court has jurisdiction and authority on appeal to render final judgment on findings of fact by the board, sustained by evidence, and it is not required that the case be remanded to the board for further action in accordance with the opinion and judgment of that court, where the question was purely one of law and there were no further facts to be determined by the board. Code, 114-710, and see American Mutual Liability Ins. Co. v. Brock, 35 Ga. App. 772 (2) (135 S. E. 103).
3. Code 114-408 provides that "If an employee who suffers an injury in his employment has a permanent disability or has sustained a permanent injury, such as specified in section 114-406, suffered elsewhere, he shall be entitled to compensation only for the degree of incapacity which would have resulted from the later accident if the early disability or injury had not existed." This court in American Mutual Liability Ins. Co. v. Brock, 35 Ga. App. 772 (supra), held that where an employee had previously sustained, at a time when he was 8 years of age, a permanent injury to his right leg, resulting in the loss of the foot and part of the leg below the knee, and that while working for the Gainesville Cotton Mills this employee had sustained another injury to his right leg, such employee was entitled to compensation for the loss of the use of his right leg "irrespective of the previous disability or injury." The Supreme Court granted a certiorari in the above case and on certiorari ( American Mutual Liability Ins. Co. v. Brock, 165 Ga. 771, 142 S. E. 101), held that "properly construed, the language employed" in the above section "evidences an intention on the part of the General Assembly to subject employers only to liability for accidents, misfortunes, or injuries resulting to their employees during the time of service or employment, and the provisions of this section were evidently embodied in the act with this end in view," and that "if the language of" this section "be so construed as to render an employer liable for an injury accruing to an employee in his employment who has already been previously injured in a prior employment, just as if such employee had never been previously injured, the incorporation of this section in the act would be ineffectual and nugatory." The Supreme Court thereupon reversed this court. The court stated that "an employee who had already sustained a permanent injury in a former and different employment should not recover compensation for the prior loss from one who was in no way chargeable therewith" and that any other ruling would render Code 114-408 unnecessary. The court said that it was not the intention of the legislature by enacting this section "to provide a means by which an employee might collect compensation just as if he had never been injured or a previous disability had never existed, and thereby receive compensation just as if the prior injury had never been sustained." This court thereafter in the case of Wisham v. Employer's Liability Assurance Corp., 55 Ga. App. 778 (191 S. E. 489), following the decision by the Supreme Court in the Brock case, held that where the evidence showed that the claimant had been blind in his right eye for a period of more than 20 years and was employed by his employer in that condition and while engaged in his employment sustained an injury to his left eye, resulting in a loss or impairment of his vision in that eye, amounting to 35 percent of his total loss of vision in that eye, and considering the total blindness in his right eye as a 50 percent loss of vision, that such employee was not entitled to compensation for 85 percent loss of his entire vision, and that the award of the board, approved by the superior court on appeal, that the employee was only entitled to a 35 percent loss of vision in his left eye, was correct and it was not error for the superior court to approve and affirm the same on appeal.
Applying these rulings to the present case, it was error for the judge of the superior court, on appeal by the claimant and employee, to change the award of the board and to render a final judgment ruling that the loss of claimant's vision in his left eye, he being blind in his right eye at the time he was employed and which injury had nothing to do with the subsequent injury to his left eye, entitled the claimant to an award for a permanent total disability and incapacity to work, in that he was thereby totally blind, and it follows that the board did not err in the award made, which was for compensation for the loss of the use of and sight in claimant's left eye, entitling him, under the provisions of Code 114-406, to compensation.

The judgment reversing the award of the board is error.
