MINCEY v. STATE.
33130.
(82 Ga. App. 5)
(60 SE2d 389)
(1950)
Maliciously maiming cow; from Baxley City Court-- Judge Sellers. April 1, 1950.
TOWNSEND, J.
The evidence in this case is entirely circumstantial and is insufficient to exclude every reasonable hypothesis save that of the guilt of the accused.
Bud Mincey was tried and convicted in the City Court of Baxley on an accusation charging him in two counts with maiming a certain cream Jersey milk cow belonging to R. E. Loft. The jury was authorized to find from the undisputed evidence of the prosecutor that it was his cow; that it had been shot in the udder and forehead; that tracks of a barefooted person which the prosecutor described as looking like the defendant's tracks led from the front door of the house to the tobacco patch behind the defendant's house; that there were cow tracks there and it appeared from the tracks that the person in question had pushed the cow over the fence at this point; that the tracks then returned to the back door of the house; that there was nothing particular by which to identify them; that he was familiar with the defendant's tracks and had seen them before; that he went to the defendant's house about 36 hours after the shooting and was standing by the back fence; that the defendant, who had been about to leave, saw him and, returning, inquired whether he had lost something; that the defendant began to curse the prosecutor and accuse him of a former transaction between the parties concerning a school bus route; that the prosecutor then stated to the defendant that he must be the one who had shot the cow, to which the defendant replied, "Prove it, then."
The prosecutor testified that about 11:00 p.m. on the night in question he had heard shots coming from the defendant's place. Another witness who lived about 75 yards away testified as to the cow tracks, but did not hear the shoes. This witness's wife heard the shots, and placed them as coming from between the defendant's house and the house next door, owned by the defendant's father. The father denied hearing the shots, and stated that at about 11:00 that night he had sold shot to three unidentified men who told him they were hunting rabbits. The defendant made a statement denying that he had shot the cow.
(After stating the foregoing facts.) Where the State relies for conviction on circumstantial evidence alone, the proved facts must not only be consistent with the hypothesis of guilt but must exclude every other reasonable hypothesis. The facts in this case are such as to raise a strong suspicion of guilt. Except for the tracks of a barefooted person coming from the defendant's house to the place where the tracks of the cow were located, and returning to the defendant's house, there is no evidence tending to place the accused at the scene of the crime at the time of its commission. Evidence of tracks alone, where they are not shown positively to belong to the defendant, or where it is not shown that no one other than the defendant could reasonably have made the tracks, is not sufficient, without more, to exclude every other reasonable hypothesis save that of the guilt of the accused. Cummings v. State, 110 Ga. 293 (35 S. E. 117); Williams v. State, 123 Ga. 278 (51 S. E. 344).
The trial court erred in overruling the motion for a new trial on the general grounds.
H. L. Williams, Solicitor, contra.
Milton C. Grainger, for plaintiff in error.
DECIDED JUNE 28, 1950.
