COMBS v. SPURLING.
33449.
(83 Ga. App. 854)
(65 SE2d 63)
(1951)
Bail trover; from Fulton Civil Court-- Judge Robert Carpenter. November 30, 1950.
GARNER, J.
Applying the law to the undisputed facts, the trial judge did not err in directing the verdict of which complaint is made.
Harvey Combs instituted in the Civil Court of Fulton County his action in trover against John Henry Spurling to recover certain articles of personal property, to which he claimed title. An affidavit to obtain bail was executed by the plaintiff. In default of bail, the levying officer seized the property described in the petition, which was in the defendant's possession. The defendant denied liability and the case proceeded to trial before a jury in said court.
It appeared from the evidence that the plaintiff was a brother of the defendant's deceased wife and that he claimed the title to the property involved by reason of an alleged parol gift of the property to him by the defendant's wife, shortly prior to her death. The property consisted principally of the household furnishings in the home of the defendant and his wife, and there was evidence that a portion of the same had been purchased by the deceased wife in her name, during the marriage, and that a portion thereof belonged to her when she married the defendant. The property was not delivered by the wife to the plaintiff but remained in the home of the defendant and the wife. There were no children. After the wife died the plaintiff brought this trover action. The court directed a verdict for the defendant, and the plaintiff moved for a new trial, which was overruled, and he excepted to this court.
Where it appears that a husband and wife are together and there is no legal separation at the time of her death, the right to the possession of the household effects is in the husband. See Shaner Motor Company v. Williams, 36 Ga. App. 766 (138 S. E. 274). Where there are no children, the issue of a marriage, and there is no will, the husband is the sole heir of his wife. In these circumstances, the household furnishings in the marital domicile at the time of and subsequent to the wife's death are presumably the property of the surviving husband, even though the same were purchased by the wife in her own name and even though a portion thereof were acquired by the wife prior to her marriage and were retained by her after her marriage and constituted a part of the furnishings of the marital domicile.
The alleged parol gift of these furnishings by the Wife, shortly prior to her death, to her brother, the plaintiff, there being no delivery thereof to the brother, is not sufficient to establish title to the property in the brother as against the husband. See Anderson v. Baker, 1 Ga. 595; Evans v. Lipscomb, 31 Ga. 71 (3). And see Upchurch v. Upchurch, 76 Ga. App. 215 (45 S. E. 2d, 855). There is nothing in the case of Purvis v. Calvert Mortgage Co., 158 Ga. 879 (124 S. E. 702), nor in Broome v. Davis, 87 Ga. 584 (13 S. E. 749), to the contrary of what is here ruled.
The foregoing is true even though at the time of the death of the wife, the husband was not working and supporting his wife.
It follows that the trial judge did not err in directing a verdict for the defendant husband.
Judgment affirmed. MacIntyre, P. J., and Townsend, J., concur.
Louis M. Tatham, J. Richmond Garland, for defendant,
William A. Thomas, for plaintiff.
DECIDED APRIL 19, 1951. REHEARING DENIED MAY 17, 1951.
