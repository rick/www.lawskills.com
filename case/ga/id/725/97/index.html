HUNTER et al. v. THE STATE.
32915.
(81 Ga. App. 797)
(60 SE2d 187)
(1950)
Violating liquor law; from Clarke Superior Court-- Judge West. December 10, 1949.
GARDNER, J.
The court did not err in overruling the motion to suppress the evidence, and did not err in overruling the motion for a new trial for any of the reasons assigned.
The defendant was tried on a special presentment containing two counts, returned by the grand jury of Clarke County, Georgia. One count was for possessing non-tax-paid whisky and the other for illegally transporting contraband whisky. He was found guilty on each count. He filed a motion for a new trial on the general grounds and afterwards added several special grounds. The court overruled this notion. It is on this judgment that error is assigned here. At night two police officers of the City of Athens were patrolling one of the main streets of Athens. Their attention was attracted to an automobile. Their suspicions were aroused that the car was being illegally used for some purpose. There is also evidence that the defendant, who was operating this car, recognized the officers or at least recognized the car in which the officers were riding as a police car. The car which attracted the attention of the officers was turning from behind a truck which was in front of the car. The car which attracted the attention of the officers was being operated slowly and was being operated so as not to violate any city ordinance or any law. As the officers approached the car the defendant threw the car in reverse and began backing it. The officers, according to the evidence, concluded that by the conduct of the, operator of the liquor car the operator of that car was trying to make an escape from the officers. Thereupon the officers pressed the bumper of their car against the bumper of the liquor car and thus forced the liquor car against a "seven-foot high" embankment on the opposite side of the street. At this juncture, a white man by the name of Austin Dixon and a Negro boy by the name of Dean alighted from the liquor car and fled, thus making their escape for the time being. The doors to the liquor car were left open. One of the officers removed the keys from the switch and unlocked the trunk of the car and seized twenty-eight gallons of contraband whisky. On closer contact between the officers and defendant the officers recognized the car as a liquor running car and evidently the defendant recognized the officers as policemen of the City of Athens, both of them being well acquainted with each other in this sort of traffic. The defendant recognized the officers and the officers recognized the defendant in person and by name and the defendant said, "This is me, old hard-luck Cobb, there she is in the car."
So it is that if the court committed no error in overruling the motion to suppress the evidence, it committed no error in overruling the motion for a new trial on the general grounds as well as the special grounds, all of which goes to the competency of the evidence and not to the sufficiency of it to convict, if it was admissible. In our opinion the court did not err in overruling the motion to suppress the evidence and properly admitted it for the consideration of the jury.
2. There has been much written on the question presented here and counsel for the defendant files a very learned brief commenting upon the constitutional provision and decisions of this State and other States which he contends are controlling and on which he argues enthusiastically for a reversal. It would be needless for us to attempt to analyze this fine presentation and we will not attempt to do so. We will call attention to only a few decisions of this court and of the Supreme Court of this State which we think are binding on this court as precedents: Williams v. State, 100 Ga. 511 (28 S. E. 624); Dozier v. State, 107 Ga. 710 (33 S. E. 318). "There is a vast difference between searching the premises of one suspected of crime and seizing any evidence of guilt, and compelling the person under suspicion to himself produce the evidence upon which he could be convicted." Duren v. Thomasville, 125 Ga. 1 (53 S. E. 814); Evans v. State, 106 Ga. 519 (32 S. E. 659). See also McIntyre v. State, 190 Ga. 875 (11 S. E. 2d, 5, 134 A.L.R. 813), wherein Judge Jenkins in discussing this point said, "As to any such violation of the State Constitution, this court, in Calhoun v. State, 144 Ga. 679 (87 S. E. 893), by a unanimous decision held that, unless 'the accused be compelled to produce the incriminating evidence . . . articles taken from the person or premises of the accused, tending to establish his guilt of the offense of which he is charged, are admissible in evidence against him, notwithstanding the articles were discovered by an unlawful search and seizure; and this rule of evidence is not violative of the constitutional prohibition of unreasonable searches and seizures.' In that case it was further held that such evidence is not inadmissible as contravening the constitutional provision that "No person shall be compelled to give testimony tending in any manner to criminate himself." ' This ruling was based upon decisions there cited, both of the Supreme Court of the United States and of this court. This case was followed in Hysler v. State, 148 Ga. 409 (96 S. E. 884); Martin v. State, 148 Ga. 406, 407 (96 S. E. 882); Groce v. State, 148 Ga. 520 (97 S. E. 525); Johnson v. State, 152 Ga. 271 (2) (109 S. E. 662); Herndon v. State, 178 Ga. 832 (3), 844 (174 S. E. 597)."
Bentley v. State, 70 Ga. App. 490 (28 S. E. 2d, 660), is no doubt in point. In that case the officers chased the car of the defendant overtaking her several times and finally puncturing one of the tires with a pistol bullet and then blocking the roadway. The defendant alighted from the car with the switch keys in her band. One of the officers took hold of the keys and the defendant, and the officers held the keys for thirty minutes or until the sheriff arrived. Thereupon the defendant released the keys and the officers by the use of them found in the trunk of the defendant's car contraband liquor. This court held that that evidence was admissible. See the many cases cited therein. See also the more recent cases, Winston v. State, 79 Ga. App. 711 (54 S. E. 2d, 354); Polite v. State, 80 Ga. App. 835 (57 S. E. 2d, 631).
The court did not err in overruling the motion to suppress the evidence, and did not err in overruling the motion for a new trial for the reasons assigned.
Judgment affirmed. MacIntyre, P. J., and Townsend, J., concur.
D. M. Pollock, Solicitor-General, contra.
James W. Arnold, for plaintiffs in error.
DECIDED JUNE 6, 1950. REHEARING DENIED JUNE 24, 1950.
