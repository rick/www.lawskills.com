UNITED STATES FIDELITY & GUARANTY COMPANY et al. v. MESSER.
37419.
(98 Ga. App. 586)
(106 SE2d 313)
(1958)
Workmen's compensation. WhitfIeld Superior Court. Before Judge Davis. July 30, 1958.
GARDNER, Presiding Judge.
Where, as here, there is any competent evidence upon which to base an award of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation, this court is without authority to reverse a superior court which affirms such an award.
Mrs. R. C. Messer filed a claim with the State Board of Workmen's Compensation asking for compensation because of the death of her husband, Robert C. Messer. The record shows that the deceased was employed by the City of Dalton and that the United States Fidelity & Guaranty Co., Inc., was the insurance carrier. The claim was heard by a single director and resulted in an award of $25.50 a week for a period not to exceed the statutory limit of 400 weeks to the claimant for the use of the claimant and her three minor children, and further found that the insurer and insurance carrier pay $350 to be applied on the burial expenses, upon satisfactory showing. The claim was appealed to the full board of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation. The full board affirmed the findings of fact and award of the single director. Counsel for the employer and insurance carrier then appealed to the superior court where the award of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation was affirmed. It is on this judgment that the case is here for review.
It was stipulated that the deceased was an employee of the City of Dalton on the date he died, and that he earned $300 per month. There was evidence that the autopsy showed a cerebral hemorrhage caused by a brain injury; that the deceased was less capable of carrying on work under stress and strain than an ordinary person would be, that the deceased had been working on the day he died for twelve hours and that such could very well be a contributing factor in his death.
The claimant testified that she awakened her husband at about 3:30 on the morning he died; that he left home about 4 o'clock driving a pickup truck which was hard to drive; that he said he did not sleep well because he had been working late the night before but that he had to go to work because he had some work he had to do.
A fellow worker of the employer, Wilbur Patterson, testified that he worked with the deceased on the day he died; that they worked raising gas valve boxes, and straightening them up by putting bricks underneath them so that the valve keys could be put in the boxes to cut off the gas in case of an emergency; that they pursued this work until quitting time sometime between 4:30 and 5 in the afternoon, when the deceased had to take some papers to the office; that the deceased drove a truck; that the deceased used a tape to measure and at times got down on his knees to place bricks in order to even up the valve boxes.
Another witness testified that he worked with the deceased and that sometimes the deceased and the witness would work until 7:30 or 8 at night measuring up pipe lines, listing fillings, etc.
The Superintendent of Utilities of the City of Dalton testified that the deceased was not required to get to work until 8 a.m. and he could leave at 5 p.m. but that the deceased was the "type of fellow" who worked a lot more than the required hours; that it was customary for the deceased to come by the house of the superintendent to discuss work and that it was reasonable to assume that this is what the deceased did on the day he was found dead in his truck in front of the house of the witness.
It is our opinion that under the record before us there was evidence upon which the State Board of Workmen's Compensation could and did make a findings of fact and award in favor of the claimant, which was affirmed by the Superior court.
The court did not err in affirming the award of the State Board of Workmen's Compensation.
Judgment affirmed. Townsend and Carlisle, JJ., concur.
Irwin, Williams, Smith & Keen, R. Beverly Irwin, contra.
Hardin, McCamy & Minor, Carlton McCamy, for plaintiffs in error.
DECIDED NOVEMBER 10, 1958.
